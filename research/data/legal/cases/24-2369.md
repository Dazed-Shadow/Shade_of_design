# Barbara Lee Chafin v. Office of Personnel Management, No. 24-2369 (Fed. Cir. May 27, 2026)

## 1. The Issue

Two issues, one legal and one factual: (1) Whether the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB) erred as a matter of law in declining to consider Chafin's inability to commute (driving, lack of public transit, prohibitive ride-share cost) when determining whether she could "render useful and efficient service" in her position under the Federal Employees' Retirement System (FERS) disability standard, 5 U.S.C. § 8451(a)(1)(B); (2) whether the Board's factual finding that Chafin was not unable to render useful and efficient service was adequately supported by the evidence — an argument the petitioner concedes is barred from Federal Circuit review under 5 U.S.C. § 8461(d).

## 2. Rule Mapping

**Controlling statute (FERS disability eligibility):** 5 U.S.C. § 8451(a)(1)(B) — "an employee shall be considered disabled only if the employee is found by [OPM] to be unable, because of disease or injury, to render useful and efficient service in the employee's position."

**Adjacent statutory provision (reassignment refusal):** 5 U.S.C. § 8451(a)(2)(A) — an otherwise-disabled employee is ineligible for benefits if the employee declines a reasonable reassignment offer that is (i) at the same grade/pay level, (ii) "within the employee's commuting area," and (iii) "one in which the employee would be able to render useful and efficient service."

**Statutory bar on factual review:** 5 U.S.C. § 8461(d) — OPM's factual findings relating to physical disability determinations "are final and conclusive and not subject to review." The Federal Circuit may address "whether there has been a substantial departure from important procedural rights, a misconstruction of the governing legislation, or some like error going to the heart of the administrative determination" (*Anthony v. OPM*, 58 F.3d 620 (Fed. Cir. 1995), quoting *Lindahl v. OPM*, 470 U.S. 768 (1985)).

**Governing canon (statutory construction):** "Where Congress includes particular language in one section of a statute but omits it in another section of the same Act, it is generally presumed that Congress acts intentionally and purposely in the disparate inclusion or exclusion" (*Russello v. United States*, 464 U.S. 16 (1983)).

**Standard of review for Board decisions:** 5 U.S.C. § 7703(c) — affirm unless arbitrary, capricious, abuse of discretion, contrary to law, obtained without required procedures, or unsupported by substantial evidence.

## 3. Synthesis: The "Why" and "So What"

**The Why:** The panel applies the *Russello* negative-implication canon to a clean two-provision statutory pair. Section 8451(a)(1)(B) — the disability-eligibility definition — speaks only of the employee's ability to "render useful and efficient service" in the position; it makes no reference to the employee's ability to commute. Section 8451(a)(2)(A) — the immediately-following reassignment-refusal provision — uses nearly identical "render useful and efficient service" language but adds an explicit "within the employee's commuting area" requirement. The textual juxtaposition is exact: Congress used the same operative phrase in both provisions but added the commute-area constraint only in the reassignment context. Under *Russello*, that disparate inclusion is presumed intentional, and considerations of commuting ability are therefore excluded from the § 8451(a)(1)(B) disability determination. Chafin's factual argument (her supervisor checked a box stating "accommodation is not possible due to the severity of the medical condition") is statutorily barred from Federal Circuit review under § 8461(d) — only the Board may review such factual matters.

**The So What:** The opinion narrows the FERS disability framework on the front end (eligibility) by excluding social-economic factors that affect the employee's ability to *reach* the workplace. Chafin's argument was that her medical inability to drive, combined with the lack of public transit and unaffordable ride-share alternatives, effectively rendered her unable to perform the position — even if she could perform the work itself once present. The panel rejects this composition: the "useful and efficient service in the employee's position" inquiry is bounded by the position itself, not by the path to it. Practical consequence: FERS disability applicants whose medical conditions create commuting barriers but not in-position work barriers must rely on the *reassignment* track (refusing an offer is statutorily relevant only at the back end) or seek accommodation through other channels (ADA-related processes, telework arrangements, etc.). The opinion's expressed sympathy ("we are sympathetic to Chafin's situation") signals the panel sees the equitable weight but is bound by the statutory architecture.

## 4. Logical Flaws & Observations

- **The Russello canon application is unusually clean here.** Many *Russello* invocations require courts to overlook plausible alternative explanations for disparate inclusion (e.g., one provision was drafted later, one borrowed from a different model, etc.). Here the two provisions are adjacent — § 8451(a)(1)(B) immediately followed by § 8451(a)(2)(A) — and use almost identical "render useful and efficient service" language. The negative-implication argument is therefore unusually strong. This is a textbook example worth knowing for statutory-construction practice.

- **The panel's sympathy paragraph is doing real work.** Lourie's "we are sympathetic to Chafin's situation. But the relevant provision ... does not provide for accommodation based on Chafin's inability to commute due to economic circumstances or proximity to her job" is a familiar appellate move — the "we can't reach this even though we'd like to" framing. It's both a signal to Congress (if you want commuting considerations in the disability inquiry, amend § 8451(a)(1)(B)) and a hedge against criticism (we recognize the human stakes but are bound by the statute).

- **The interplay with the underlying misconduct removal is unmentioned but contextually significant.** Chafin was removed in July 2018 for "under the influence while on duty" misconduct after a December 2016 finding. She filed for FERS disability retirement in April 2019 with a retroactive start date of February 2018 — i.e., she sought to recast her termination as a disability retirement. The opinion handles this neutrally and rules purely on the statutory ground, but the procedural posture (disability application following misconduct removal) is a recurring pattern in FERS cases that doctrinally invites scrutiny.

- **The § 8461(d) factual-review bar is a noteworthy jurisdictional limit.** The Federal Circuit has explicit statutory authority over Board decisions under 28 U.S.C. § 1295(a)(9), but § 8461(d) carves out factual findings relating to physical disability determinations specifically. This creates an unusual division of labor: the Federal Circuit reviews legal/procedural questions, but the Board has effectively final word on the *factual* disability determination itself. Worth knowing for any Federal-Circuit-bound MSPB appeal — frame challenges as legal/procedural, not evidentiary.

## 5. Follow-ups for Mr. C

- **LSAT reasoning angle (canon application):** This case is a near-pristine application of the *Russello* canon. The structure: (1) two adjacent statutory provisions, (2) shared operative phrase ("render useful and efficient service"), (3) one provision includes an additional element ("commuting area"), (4) court concludes Congress intended that additional element only where written. Memorize this as the prototype for negative-implication arguments in statutory construction. Practice exercise: identify three other places in federal statutes where adjacent provisions share operative language but differ in one specific qualifier — the negative-implication argument will be available in each.

- **LSAT reasoning angle (jurisdictional limits):** The § 8461(d) factual-review bar is a classic example of a statutory withdrawal of jurisdiction over a *type* of question rather than a *category* of case. Compare to: § 314(d) IPR-institution bar (from [[24-1600|Hafeman v. Google]]), the Supreme Court's "no judicial review of agency discretion" doctrines under APA § 701, etc. There's a family of provisions that pull *fact-finding* out of appellate review while leaving *law-applying* in. Worth tracing this family for cross-doctrinal pattern recognition.

- **Doctrinal-evolution thread:** The FERS disability framework was modeled on the older Civil Service Retirement System (CSRS) provisions. Most CSRS/FERS interpretation flows through *Lindahl v. OPM*, 470 U.S. 768 (1985), and *Anthony v. OPM*, 58 F.3d 620 (Fed. Cir. 1995) — both worth knowing for any disability-retirement question.

- **Cross-stitch with prior corpus:** Compare with [[24-1600|Hafeman v. Google]] (§ 314(d) IPR institution-decision bar) and [[24-2316|IRAdvocates v. Mullin]] (§ 307 Tariff Act administrative-enforcement procedural rights). All three cases involve federal courts policing the boundary between what's appellate-reviewable and what's agency-final. The structural pattern across all three: statutory text creating a limited window for judicial review, with the appellate court enforcing the limits even where it expresses sympathy for the petitioner.

- **Watch thread:** Whether Chafin or similarly-situated FERS applicants pursue ADA-based or telework-policy theories as alternative remedies. The opinion explicitly says § 8451(a)(1)(B) "does not provide for accommodation based on Chafin's inability to commute due to economic circumstances" — leaving open whether other statutory schemes might.

## Main Opinion Summary

Judge Lourie, for a unanimous panel of Moore (Chief Judge), Mayer, and Lourie, affirms the Merit Systems Protection Board's denial of Chafin's FERS disability retirement application. **Statutory construction:** 5 U.S.C. § 8451(a)(1)(B) defines disability eligibility solely in terms of the employee's ability to "render useful and efficient service in the employee's position" — without reference to commuting ability. The adjacent § 8451(a)(2)(A) reassignment-refusal provision uses nearly identical language but adds an explicit "within the employee's commuting area" element. Under the *Russello* canon of disparate inclusion, Congress's choice to add the commuting reference only to the reassignment provision implies Congress intended to exclude commuting considerations from the disability eligibility determination. The Board therefore correctly declined to consider Chafin's inability to drive, the lack of public transit, and the prohibitive cost of ride-sharing services. **Factual review barred:** Chafin's alternative argument — that her supervisor's certification stating accommodation was not possible established her disability — is barred from Federal Circuit review by 5 U.S.C. § 8461(d). Only the Board may review such factual underpinnings of physical disability determinations. Panel notes sympathy for Chafin's situation but is bound by the statutory framework. AFFIRMED.
