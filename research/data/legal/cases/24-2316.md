# International Rights Advocates v. Mullin, No. 24-2316 (Fed. Cir. June 5, 2026)

## 1. The Issue

Whether an advocacy organization establishes Article III standing to compel agency enforcement action by alleging that the government's unlawful withholding or unreasonable delay of action on the organization's petitions (here, urging Customs to investigate forced child labor in Côte d'Ivoire cocoa imports under § 307 of the Tariff Act of 1930) caused (a) financial harm requiring additional investigations and lobbying expenditures, and (b) denial of a major litigation tool in the organization's mission arsenal.

## 2. Rule Mapping

**Controlling statute:** Article III of the U.S. Constitution (standing requirement); 5 U.S.C. § 701 et seq. (Administrative Procedure Act); 19 U.S.C. § 1307 (§ 307 of the Tariff Act of 1930, prohibiting importation of goods produced with forced or child labor); 19 C.F.R. § 12.42 (implementing regulation governing Customs petitions and investigations).

**Governing standing test (Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U.S. 555 (1992)):** Injury-in-fact + causation + redressability. Injury must be "concrete and particularized" and "actual or imminent, not conjectural or hypothetical."

**Organizational-standing doctrine arc:**
- *Havens Realty Corp. v. Coleman*, 455 U.S. 363 (1982) — Organization establishes standing where defendant's conduct "directly unraveled and frustrated" its core services, "requiring [organization] to expend significant resources to identify and counteract" the defendant's actions.
- *Food & Drug Admin. v. Alliance for Hippocratic Medicine*, 602 U.S. 367 (2024) — Narrows Havens: "An organization that has not suffered a concrete injury caused by a defendant's action cannot spend its way into standing simply by expending money to gather information and advocate against the defendant's action."
- *Abigail Alliance for Better Access to Developmental Drugs v. Eschenbach*, 469 F.3d 129 (D.C. Cir. 2006) — Survives motion to dismiss by alleging diversion of resources from "counseling, referral, advocacy, and educational" customer-service activities.

**Procedural-rights standing:** "[T]he grant of a procedural right alone . . . cannot serve as the basis for Article III standing unless the procedures in question are designed to protect some threatened concrete interest of petitioners that is the ultimate basis of [their] standing" (*US Inventor, Inc. v. USPTO*, 156 F.4th 1306 (Fed. Cir. 2025), quoting *Gettman v. DEA*, 290 F.3d 430 (D.C. Cir. 2002)).

## 3. Synthesis: The "Why" and "So What"

**The Why:** The Federal Circuit affirms the Trade Court's dismissal because IRAdvocates failed to plead the type of concrete impediment to its actual customer-service activities that *Havens* and *Hippocratic Medicine* require. Post-*Hippocratic Medicine*, "spending money to gather information and advocate" — even substantial money over multiple years — is constitutionally insufficient when those expenditures are self-directed in support of the organization's social-policy mission rather than responsive to a defendant's interference with the organization's operational services. IRAdvocates' alleged injuries (extra investigations, supplemental petitions, drafting communications) were "voluntary, self-inflicted, and directed toward advocating against Customs' inaction" — they did not allege that Customs' inaction prevented IRAdvocates from making educational documentaries, representing individuals in litigation, or supporting members' ethical-chocolate market position. As to the procedural-rights theory, § 307 confers a right to petition Customs but does not protect any underlying concrete interest of petitioners as such.

**The So What:** This opinion materially raises the pleading bar for advocacy-organization standing in agency-inaction litigation. The opinion supplies an unusually explicit roadmap of what IRAdvocates *could have* pleaded — that members produce more costly ethical chocolate underpriced by infringers, or that ethical certifications were tarnished by inadequate monitoring — which functions both as harmless dicta and as litigation guidance for the inevitable repleaded complaint. Advocacy organizations challenging agency under-enforcement now face a structural problem: the more their resources go into producing the very evidence the agency demands (here, fresh on-the-ground investigations), the more those expenditures look like self-inflicted advocacy spending rather than impediments to a separate operational mission.

## 4. Logical Flaws & Observations

- **The doctrinal tension between *Havens* and *Hippocratic Medicine* is genuinely unresolved.** Havens' "drain on the organization's resources" language is now read through a Hippocratic-Medicine filter that requires the underlying activity drained from to be operational (counseling, referral, customer service) rather than mission-coextensive (advocacy, research, lobbying). Where a mission-driven advocacy organization's *core activity* IS advocacy, the test creates a near-impossible standing burden — exactly the result Hippocratic Medicine seems to intend, but uncomfortable for the original Havens framework.
- **The opinion's footnote 2 narrows IRAdvocates' best argument.** The court notes that "nearly all the expenses identified by IRAdvocates" occurred *before* Customs notified them their information was stale — meaning IRAdvocates can't even argue Customs' staleness notification *induced* the expenditure. But this slices very thinly: a multi-year campaign of supplemental petitions, by definition, will accrue expenses on both sides of any single agency communication. The footnote is designed to rebut a foreseeable counter-argument but the doctrinal cut is somewhat strained.
- **The procedural-rights ruling is doing significant background work.** The court holds that § 307's right-to-petition does not "inherently protect any interest of the petitioners" — meaning the petition right is a freestanding procedural grant, not a vindication of a concrete interest. This is consistent with Consumer Watchdog (Fed. Cir. 2014) but creates a structural problem for any beneficiary-style standing theory in agency-inaction litigation: if the only "right" you have is to file a petition, denial of action on that petition gives you nothing.
- **The government's late-stage representation** at oral argument that the investigation is "not active right now . . . but . . . it is still pending" and not bounded by a time limit is a remarkable preservation of agency discretion. The Federal Circuit accepts this representation without skepticism, but the practical effect is that an investigation can remain "pending" indefinitely with no judicial review of the delay — a structural protection of executive enforcement discretion.

## 5. Follow-ups for Mr. C

- **LSAT reasoning angle:** Standing doctrine is heavy in conditional reasoning ("if A and B and C, then standing exists; the plaintiff must establish each of A, B, and C"). This case tests injury-in-fact specifically. Practice exercise: trace the court's reasoning for *why* IRAdvocates' alleged injury fails — it's a "type of injury" argument (their injury is the wrong type) rather than a "magnitude of injury" argument (their injury isn't big enough). What is the formal logical difference?
- **Doctrinal-evolution thread:** Hippocratic Medicine is recent (2024) and lower courts are still working out its reach. The Federal Circuit's narrow reading of Havens here is one data point; circuit-split potential exists, especially in the D.C. Circuit (which decided Abigail Alliance pre-Hippocratic-Medicine). Worth tracking for next 12-24 months.
- **Cross-domain connection:** This is the only non-patent case in your current corpus. The standing analysis applies across all federal litigation; the substantive Tariff Act / forced-labor framework is a discrete domain. For LSAT practice, the standing analysis is the transferable skill; for legal-domain breadth, the human-rights/trade angle is the novel content. Worth flagging that the Federal Circuit's docket is much broader than just patents.

## Main Opinion Summary

Judge Stoll, for a panel of Reyna, Taranto, and Stoll, affirms the Court of International Trade's dismissal of IRAdvocates' complaint for lack of organizational standing. Two alleged injuries are addressed: (1) financial harm from investigating forced child labor and seeking § 307 enforcement, and (2) denial of a major litigation tool. As to the first, the court applies the *Alliance for Hippocratic Medicine* framework and holds that IRAdvocates' resource expenditures were voluntary advocacy directed at Customs' inaction, not impediments to operational customer-service activities of the type that supported standing in *Havens* and *Abigail Alliance*. Unlike housing-counseling services or terminally-ill-patient referral services, IRAdvocates' core activities (research, public education, lobbying) are themselves the channel through which the alleged injury manifests — making the diverted resources self-inflicted advocacy spending rather than concrete impediment. The court explicitly lists pleading-paths IRAdvocates did not pursue (ethical-chocolate price disadvantage, certification tarnishment) as illustrations of what *would* have sufficed. As to the second, the procedural-rights argument is forfeited (not pleaded below) and, alternatively, fails on the merits because § 307's right to petition does not protect an underlying concrete interest of the petitioner under *US Inventor* and *Consumer Watchdog*. AFFIRMED.
