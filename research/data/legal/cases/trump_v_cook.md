# Trump v. Cook, No. 25A312 (U.S. June 29, 2026)

## 1. The Issue

Whether a District Court's preliminary injunction restoring Lisa Cook — a Federal Reserve Governor whom the President purported to fire for alleged pre-office mortgage fraud — to her position pending merits litigation should be stayed. Three sub-questions carry the analysis: **(1)** whether the Federal Reserve Act's "for cause" removal provision (12 U.S.C. § 242) is judicially reviewable at all; **(2)** whether the statute implicitly requires **notice + opportunity to respond** before removal, given its fixed-14-year-term structure; and **(3)** whether federal courts have **equitable authority** to enter interim reinstatement relief against the President's removal of a principal executive officer.

Underlying the whole case: whether the Federal Reserve — decided the same day in the companion case *Trump v. Slaughter* — falls within a **historical-tradition carveout** to that day's newly-announced general rule that principal executive officers must be removable at will.

## 2. Rule Mapping

**Statutory removal provision (Federal Reserve Act, 12 U.S.C. § 242):** "Each member shall hold office for a term of fourteen years from the expiration of the term of his predecessor, unless sooner removed for cause by the President." The word "cause" is undefined; the statute is silent as to procedure.

**Common-law "cause" as term of art (backdrop rule):** When Congress legislates "against the backdrop of the common law" — *Comcast Corp. v. National Assn. of African American-Owned Media*, 589 U.S. 327, 335 (2020) — courts read common-law terms per their settled common-law meaning. *Jam v. International Finance Corp.*, 586 U.S. 199, 211 (2019). "Cause" at common law required more than "any reason" — it required a "substantial, reasonable and just" ground amounting to a "disqualification" akin to inefficiency or incompetency. *In re Nichols*, 6 Abb. N. Cas. 474, 480 (NY Sup. Ct. 1879); *Board of Street Comm'rs of Hagerstown v. Williams*, 96 Md. 232, 236–239, 53 A. 923, 924–925 (1903). Contours depend on the "nature of the service to be performed" and "duties of the office."

**Fixed-term + for-cause = implicit notice-and-hearing (Reagan/Shurtleff rule):** "[T]he rule . . . is that notice and hearing are essential before an officer's removal where the term of office is for a fixed period." *Reagan v. United States*, 182 U.S. 419, 425 (1901); *Shurtleff v. United States*, 189 U.S. 311, 314 (1903). Congress is presumed to legislate against this baseline. *Williams v. Taylor*, 529 U.S. 420, 434 (2000). Minimal process required: **notice of charges + some opportunity to respond** (written materials sufficient; no full judicial trial). *Londoner v. City and County of Denver*, 210 U.S. 373, 386 (1908).

**Central-bank historical tradition (Federal Reserve carveout):** The Federal Reserve follows in a "distinct historical tradition" of independent central banking traced from the Bank of North America (1781) → First Bank of the United States (1791) → Second Bank of the United States (1816) → Federal Reserve System (1913, restructured 1933 and 1935). All three predecessors featured for-cause-equivalent protection from Presidential control, animated by Founders' fear (Hamilton in *Report on a National Bank* 331) of "suspicion" of political manipulation of monetary policy. *Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau*, 591 U.S. 197, 222, n. 8 (2020) (naming Federal Reserve as example of an entity that "may not be encompassed by *Myers*'s general rule").

**Equity — pending-litigation protection of *de facto* officer:** Courts of equity historically could not adjudicate final title to office (a quo warranto or mandamus function of courts of law), but *could* "recogniz[e] and protec[t] the possession of officers de facto, . . . pending a litigation" at law "to determine their title." 2 J. High, *Law of Injunctions* § 1315, at 866 (2d ed. 1880). Distinct from *In re Sawyer*, 124 U.S. 200 (1888), and *White v. Berry*, 171 U.S. 366 (1898), which foreclosed final equitable relief where mandamus/quo warranto were adequate legal remedies.

**Stay standard:** Applicant must show (i) likelihood of success on merits, (ii) likelihood of certiorari grant, (iii) irreparable harm, (iv) balance of equities. *Hollingsworth v. Perry*, 558 U.S. 183, 190 (2010) (per curiam); *Nken v. Holder*, 556 U.S. 418, 434 (2009).

## 3. Synthesis: The "Why" and "So What"

**The Why.** The majority (Roberts, C.J., for a 5-Justice Court) declines to grant the stay on the **narrowest possible ground**: the President did not afford Cook the pre-termination procedural protections that the Federal Reserve Act — read against the *Reagan/Shurtleff* backdrop — implicitly requires. That resolution is deliberately narrow: the Court explicitly avoids the broader constitutional question of whether *any* limitation on the President's removal power over Federal Reserve Governors is permissible, because *the Government did not challenge the statute's constitutionality* in this application. This is a rare instance of the Court honoring party presentation limits at the emergency-docket stage — Alito's dissent (joined by Gorsuch) and Barrett's dissent both criticize the majority for going too far even so; Thomas's dissent criticizes it for not going far enough (would hold the statute itself unconstitutional under Article II).

The three-part structure of the majority's holding is worth tracing because each part is a distinct doctrinal move:

- **Reviewability** (Part II-A). The Government argued the President's "cause" determination is *wholly* unreviewable — a textual commitment to Presidential discretion. The Court rejects this. Congress *could* have written the statute to make cause unreviewable ("Congress could of course afford the President the power to remove Federal Reserve Governors at will. Or Congress could exempt the President's removal of Governors for cause from judicial review. But Congress has done neither."). The Government's own concession that courts may review removals where the President "identifies no cause at all" defeats the position: **any judicial line-drawing requires statutory interpretation of what "cause" means**, which is a merits inquiry, not an unreviewability rule.

- **"Cause" as term of art requiring a substantial threshold** (Part II-B). Both parties get rejected. Government argues "cause" = any concern about conduct/ability/fitness/competence (too low). Cook argues "cause" = only the specific statutory grounds Congress used in *other* removal statutes as of 1935: inefficiency, neglect of duty, malfeasance, ineligibility (too high). The Court chooses **structural context over both extremes**: cause "must reflect the Federal Reserve's unique historical status and role," which counsels a *substantial* threshold. Whether cause exists depends on the **seriousness of the alleged misconduct** and the **extent of any nexus to the Governor's professional duties**. Key structural insight: "any perceived or alleged misstep (past or present) could provide a ready pretext for a Governor's removal — a fact that he would surely know, and that would surely weigh on him as he decided what to say and how to vote. Nothing could be more corrosive of the independence that Congress sought to preserve."

- **Notice + hearing implicit in fixed-term + for-cause** (Part III-A). This is the *Reagan/Shurtleff* rebuttable presumption applied straight: when Congress enacts a fixed-term + for-cause structure "against the backdrop of the common law," it incorporates the common-law rule requiring pre-termination notice and opportunity to respond. What's remarkable is Thomas's dissent characterizing this as a "single line of dicta" from two 1901/1903 cases; the majority responds that vertical stare decisis makes a Supreme Court statement about "the rule" load-bearing whether or not the officers in *Reagan* and *Shurtleff* ultimately won.

**The federal-reserve-tradition move** (Part III-A(2), Kavanaugh concurrence) is where *Cook* has to be read with *Slaughter*. Same-day, the *Slaughter* majority (also Roberts, C.J.) overrules *Humphrey's Executor* and holds that principal executive officers must generally be removable at will. But the *Slaughter* majority preserves a specific carveout for the Federal Reserve "to the extent that it follows in the distinct historical tradition of the First and Second Banks of the United States." *Cook* activates that carveout: the Federal Reserve's "founding-era pedigree" — Hamilton's *Report on a National Bank* + First Bank's private-direction structure + Second Bank's independence from Presidential control — constitutes the "essential content" that history provides "to undefined provisions in the frame of our government" (quoting Frankfurter's *Youngstown* concurrence). Kavanaugh's concurrence is explicit: after *Slaughter*, "there is a clear choice: Either the Federal Reserve may remain independent (with the Governors removable for cause, not at will), or it may not." He would not "risk destabilizing the U.S. economy" to leave that open.

**The So What.** Three propositions land as usable precedent:

1. **The Federal Reserve is constitutionally distinct.** After *Cook* + *Slaughter*, the Federal Reserve's for-cause protection is not just statutorily valid — it is constitutionally *sanctioned by history*. This is a **historical-tradition safe harbor** carved out of the *Slaughter* rule that Article II requires at-will removal of principal executive officers. Other agencies claiming similar protection will need to show the same kind of founding-era pedigree; nothing else in the majority opinion suggests the carveout extends beyond central banking.

2. **Fixed-term + for-cause statutes carry implicit procedural requirements.** *Reagan/Shurtleff* is reaffirmed and applied: Congress cannot enact a fixed-term-with-cause structure and simultaneously permit summary removal. Any officer whose statute has that structure (and there are still many — see Thomas's dissent Note 2 cataloging hundreds) has a pre-termination notice-and-hearing right *even if the statute doesn't say so explicitly*. This is a durable statutory-interpretation holding independent of the Federal Reserve carveout.

3. **Equity can protect de facto officers pending litigation.** *In re Sawyer* and *White v. Berry* are cabined to their facts (adequate legal remedies foreclosed equity there); interim reinstatement injunctions are permissible where the officer has a likely meritorious claim. Practical effect: officers wrongfully fired can seek and receive preliminary injunctions preserving their office during litigation, at least where Article III standing and statutory rights exist.

Downstream: expect litigation over *which* other agencies (if any) can claim the Federal Reserve's historical-tradition carveout. Thomas's dissent argues sharply that even the Federal Reserve doesn't qualify because it "is not a 'bank' but a novel federal agency" — that critique may resurface if a future case tests, say, the FDIC or the OCC.

## 4. Logical Flaws & Observations

- **Party presentation at emergency-docket altitude.** The most striking structural feature of *Cook* is what the majority *doesn't* decide. The Government did not challenge the constitutionality of § 242's removal restriction. The majority accepts that concession and resolves the case on statutory grounds. Barrett's dissent argues the majority still goes too broadly (naming the historical-tradition carveout in Part III-A(2) despite the constitutional question being waived); Alito's dissent goes further, arguing the majority should not have opined on any issue beyond the two the D.C. Circuit resolved (due process + "cause" scope). This tension — between judicial restraint and interim-docket transparency — is the same tension Barrett named in *Mirabelli v. Bonta*, 607 U.S. 492 (2026): "lock-in risk of saying too much against the transparency cost of saying too little." *Cook* comes down closer to the "saying enough to be useful" side, and the meta-honesty note is that reasonable minds can disagree.

- **The "cause" definition the Court leaves open.** The majority "need not fully demarcate the contours of 'cause' today." That's *In re Nichols*-style deferential language, but it means the substantive standard remains to be fleshed out on remand. Lower courts now have to apply a "substantial threshold" reflecting the Federal Reserve's role — likely a two-step analysis: (a) seriousness of misconduct; (b) nexus to professional duties. Whether *Cook*'s alleged pre-office mortgage fraud clears that bar is the merits question the majority carefully does *not* resolve.

- **Thomas's Article II theory as inverse counterpoint.** Thomas's dissent is the pure Article II unitary-executive position: removal power is inherent in the vesting clause; Congress cannot limit it; the statute is unconstitutional. The dissent's historical work is thorough but faces the problem the majority names: the historical tradition of central bank independence *predates* the Constitution (Bank of North America 1781) and was reaffirmed by the same Founders who ratified Article II. This is one of the few areas where founding-era practice cuts *against* a strong unitary-executive reading, and Thomas's response — that the Federal Reserve isn't really analogous to the First/Second Banks — is precisely the fact-intensive line the majority declines to draw at this stage.

- **The equitable-relief holding is quietly load-bearing.** *In re Sawyer* and *White v. Berry* had been read broadly to foreclose equitable relief in officer-removal cases. *Cook* clarifies that these cases addressed *final* equitable relief where legal remedies (mandamus, quo warranto, backpay) were adequate — they do not foreclose *interim* protection of officers pending litigation. This preserves preliminary-injunction practice for wrongfully-removed officers, which had been in real doubt after *Wilcox* and *Boyle* stay orders.

- **The stale-hash procedural detail worth naming.** The dispute over notice-and-hearing carries a substantive undercurrent: the majority (Part III-B) rejects the Government's argument that Trump's social-media post ("Cook must resign, now!!!") constituted adequate notice. What "will not do" is a post that "did not suggest that a response from Cook would be appropriate, nor did it even provide a clear account of the charge made against her." The rule that emerges: notice must include (i) some explanation of evidence at issue, (ii) some avenue for response, (iii) a deadline. *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co.*, 339 U.S. 306, 314–315 (1950) is invoked by analogy. This is the shape of due process bleeding into statutory-notice doctrine.

## 5. Follow-ups for Mr. C

- **Doctrinal-scaffold angle (for JR's returning-paralegal register):** The core structural move in *Cook* is a **presumption + backdrop-rebuttal** pattern. Congress enacts fixed-term + for-cause → *presumed* to legislate against the common-law backdrop (*Comcast*, *Jam*) → common-law backdrop is *Reagan/Shurtleff*'s notice-and-hearing rule → therefore statute implicitly requires notice-and-hearing. This is the same shape as the FIAFEA presumption-of-review rebuttal analysis in [[24-1990|Jacobson v. United States]] and the "true nature" doctrine in [[24-2242|Erdemir v. ITC]] — Congress presumed to write against a settled backdrop, backdrop displaced only by clear-and-convincing showing to the contrary. **Worth naming as a discrete category:** "backdrop rebuttable presumptions" as a distinct interpretive doctrine.

- **Weight-of-words angle:** "For cause" carries centuries of common-law weight — it is not a phrase Congress uses lightly. The majority's move is to make that weight legible: "Congress enacted this statute 'against the backdrop of the common law,' such that we must look to the common law to decipher what 'cause' (a term of art) requires." Two words with statutory weight; two centuries of case law loaded into them. This is the same discipline as reading "true nature" (Erdemir), "arbitrary and capricious" (APA), or "reasonable" (contract law) — none of which mean what plain-English reading would suggest. **The lesson worth naming:** statutory terms with common-law pedigrees are never plain-English; they carry their history.

- **Structural analogy (rigid statutes, flexible analysis):** *Cook* is a beautiful example of the rigid-in-statutes-but-flexible-in-analysis pattern you named in your legal-background reflection. The statutory language is fixed ("for cause"), but its application to the Federal Reserve's unique historical role is deeply flexible — the "substantial threshold" the Court identifies depends on the "nature of the service to be performed" and "duties of the office." Same phrase, different bite in different institutional contexts. Compare to the Federal Circuit's willingness to distinguish *Fisher* in [[24-1990|Jacobson]] based on whether jurisdiction blends with merits — same doctrinal source, differently binding depending on structural shape.

- **The historical-tradition safe harbor as a new (or newly-explicit) doctrine.** *Slaughter* announces a general rule (Article II requires at-will removal of principal executive officers) but preserves specific historical-tradition carveouts (Federal Reserve; non-Article III courts reserved). *Cook* activates the Federal Reserve carveout. This is a doctrinal shape worth naming because it will recur: "general rule + historical-tradition carveout" is how the Court is going to handle other structural-Constitution questions where founding-era practice cuts against modern theory. Watch for it in future terms.

- **Cross-stitch with prior corpus.** *Cook* + *Slaughter* together sit at the top of the removal-law doctrinal tree; every future case in this space (agency independence, civil service, MSPB, adjudicative bodies) will trace back here. Pair with *Free Enterprise Fund*, *Seila Law*, *Collins v. Yellen*, *Wilcox*, and *Boyle* to see the compound arc: from *Humphrey's Executor* (1935) → *Wiener* (1958) → *Free Enterprise Fund* (2010) → *Seila Law* (2020) → *Slaughter* + *Cook* (2026). **Recommend an SR Sharpens pass on this arc as a foundational doctrinal-map exercise** — it teaches Article II removal law through evolution, which is exactly how doctrine is best learned from ground zero.

## Main Opinion Summary

Chief Justice Roberts, writing for a five-Justice majority (joined by Sotomayor, Kagan, Kavanaugh, and Jackson), denies the President's application for a stay of the D.C. District Court's preliminary injunction restoring Governor Lisa Cook to her position on the Federal Reserve Board. In August 2025, President Trump purported to fire Cook — the first Federal Reserve Governor fired in the central bank's 111-year history — for alleged mortgage fraud committed shortly before she assumed office. The District Court enjoined the removal on due process and "for cause" statutory grounds; the D.C. Circuit refused a stay. **The Court holds:** (1) The Government has not shown likelihood of success on the merits. Federal Reserve Act § 242's "for cause" language is judicially reviewable (rejecting the Government's textual-commitment argument); "cause" is a common-law term of art requiring a substantial threshold reflecting the Federal Reserve's unique historical role; the President's determination is not immune from review. (2) On the narrowest available ground, the President failed to afford Cook the notice-and-opportunity-to-respond that fixed-term + for-cause statutes implicitly require under the *Reagan/Shurtleff* rule. Trump's social-media post did not constitute constitutionally adequate notice. (3) Federal courts have equitable authority to grant interim reinstatement pending merits litigation — *In re Sawyer* and *White v. Berry* addressed final equitable relief where legal remedies were adequate, not interim protection of *de facto* officers. (4) The Federal Reserve's statutory removal restriction is constitutional under Article II because the Federal Reserve follows in the "distinct historical tradition" of the First and Second Banks of the United States — an independence "sanctioned by history" that the Framers themselves guaranteed against Presidential control. Concurring opinions by Kavanaugh (emphasizing the risk of leaving the Federal Reserve's status open post-*Slaughter*) and Jackson (relying primarily on equitable factors). Dissents by Thomas (Article II forecloses any removal restriction on principal officers; statute is unconstitutional; equity cannot interfere with removal), Alito joined by Gorsuch (majority reached too many issues on an underdeveloped record), and Barrett (concurring in some critiques of scope; also arguing the injunction is broader than the majority acknowledges). **Application for stay DENIED.** Decided companion to [[trump_v_slaughter|Trump v. Slaughter]] the same day.
