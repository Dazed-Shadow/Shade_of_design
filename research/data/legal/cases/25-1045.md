# Ollnova Technologies Ltd. v. ecobee Technologies ULC, Nos. 25-1045, 25-1046 (Fed. Cir. June 4, 2026)

## 1. The Issue

Multiple consolidated issues in this patent infringement appeal involving four building-automation patents: (1) Whether the district court abused its discretion by submitting a single combined infringement question covering all four asserted patents, in light of the Seventh Amendment unanimity requirement; (2) whether the jury instructions and verdict form on Alice § 101 step two for the '495 patent erred by failing to identify the abstract idea or instruct the jury that the abstract idea itself cannot supply the inventive concept; (3) whether the asserted claims of the '887 and '371 patents are directed to abstract ideas under Alice step one; (4) whether substantial evidence supports the jury's infringement finding as to the '371 patent's repeat-communication limitation.

## 2. Rule Mapping

**Seventh Amendment:** "Unanimity in jury verdicts is required where . . . the Seventh Amendment applies" (*Andres v. United States*, 333 U.S. 740 (1948)). In multi-patent cases, the verdict form "needed to have included, at the very least, separate infringement questions for each asserted patent" to ensure the jury was unanimous as to which patent was infringed (*Optis Cellular Tech., LLC v. Apple Inc.*, 139 F.4th 1363 (Fed. Cir. 2025)).

**Alice § 101 framework (Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank Int'l, 573 U.S. 208 (2014)):**
- **Step one:** Are the claims directed to a patent-ineligible concept (abstract idea, law of nature, natural phenomenon)?
- **Step two:** Do the additional elements (individually and as ordered combination) transform the claim into a patent-eligible application?
- **Key limitation:** "The abstract idea itself cannot supply the inventive concept" (*Trading Techs. Int'l, Inc. v. IBG LLC*, 921 F.3d 1378 (Fed. Cir. 2019); *BSG Tech LLC v. Buyseasons, Inc.*, 899 F.3d 1281 (Fed. Cir. 2018)).

**Section 101 eligibility for technological-improvement claims:** "An architecture providing a technological solution to a technological problem" can provide the requisite inventive concept (*Amdocs (Israel) Ltd. v. Openet Telecom, Inc.*, 841 F.3d 1288 (Fed. Cir. 2016)).

**Standard of review:** Fifth Circuit law for JMOL (*Lighting Ballast Control, LLC v. Philips Elecs. N. Am. Corp.*, 790 F.3d 1329 (Fed. Cir. 2015)); de novo for jury instructions on patent-law issues, abuse of discretion for non-patent issues (*Eko Brands*, 946 F.3d 1367; *Optis*, 139 F.4th 1363).

## 3. Synthesis: The "Why" and "So What"

**The Why:** Three holdings drive the disposition. First, on verdict-form unanimity, the court extends Optis: a single combined infringement question asking whether defendant infringed "ANY" of the asserted claims of multiple distinct patents creates an unacceptable risk that jurors agreed on liability but disagreed on which patent was infringed — violating the Seventh Amendment's unanimity requirement on each legal claim. Second, on Alice § 101 instructions, the court holds that where step two is submitted to the jury for resolution of underlying factual disputes about whether claim limitations were well-understood/routine/conventional, the jury MUST be told (i) what the abstract idea is, and (ii) that the abstract idea itself cannot supply the inventive concept. Otherwise the jury can mistakenly treat the abstract idea itself as the basis for finding the claims non-conventional. Third, on substantive § 101 eligibility, the '887 patent's specific timing-and-threshold-conditioned transmission architecture and the '371 patent's COV-aggregation-with-repeat-until-acknowledgment architecture each "recite a technological solution to a technological problem" sufficient to clear Alice step one.

**The So What:** Three procedural lessons for patent trial practice: (1) Verdict forms in multi-patent cases must break out infringement by patent, full stop — Optis was not a one-off, this is now firmly the rule. (2) When Alice step two is given to a jury, the abstract idea identification (or stipulation, or assumption) must be in the jury charge; trying to keep step one purely judicial while letting the jury wade into step two without context creates reversible error. (3) Footnote 7 (Chen, J.) is doing significant doctrinal work, preserving district courts' practice of denying motions on step-two grounds without ruling step one, but warning that the practice creates a risk of post-trial step-one identification differing from the jury-charge framing — judges "always retain discretion to determine the abstract idea before proceeding to step 2, and doing so sometime before trial can avoid the possibility that an after-trial resolution of step 1 would identify a different abstract idea than reflected in the jury charge, requiring a new trial." Read together, this opinion narrows the procedural escape valves that have allowed courts to defer step-one analysis indefinitely.

## 4. Logical Flaws & Observations

- **The verdict-form holding has efficiency-vs-rights tension.** Combining infringement questions across patents saves jury time and verdict-form complexity, particularly in cases with many asserted patents and overlapping accused features. The court's bright-line rule (patent-by-patent at minimum) trades that efficiency for unanimity certainty. The rule is correct under Andres and Optis but will materially burden multi-patent trial practice — especially where damages models don't differentiate per-patent.
- **The Alice instruction holding is more nuanced than it first appears.** The court is not requiring step one to be tried; it's requiring that whatever framing of step one the court adopts (stipulation, assumption, ruling) be communicated to the jury before the jury wades into step two. This is sensible but creates a new procedural friction: courts that previously could submit step two factually without committing to a step-one articulation now must commit — or face reversible error.
- **The § 101 eligibility analyses for the '887 and '371 patents are doctrinally cautious.** The court repeatedly cites *Packet Intelligence* and *Amdocs* for the "technological solution to technological problem" framework, but the holdings here turn on specific limitations (polling intervals + conditional transmission for '887; COV aggregation + repeat-until-ack for '371) that the court treats as escaping the "abstract result" trap of Electric Power Group, Chamberlain, and Affinity Labs. The line between "specific technique for X" (eligible) and "abstract idea of doing X" (ineligible) remains highly fact- and characterization-sensitive — and footnote 11's dismissal of ecobee's human-driven landlord analogy as "fail[ing] to account for the wireless network communications challenges" hints at how much of the analysis rests on the court's willingness to take the technology context seriously.
- **The substantial-evidence affirmance on '371 infringement is significant.** ecobee argued the claims require "the exact same message" be repeated, and the accused thermostats include additional information in retry messages. The court holds that the jury could credit expert testimony that "the same information again, at a minimum" satisfies the limitation even with additional data — meaning the claim term "the at least one change-of-value update" can include retry transmissions that contain a superset of the original. This is a generous reading favorable to patent owners but well within the jury's discretion to credit expert testimony.

## 5. Follow-ups for Mr. C

- **LSAT reasoning angle:** This opinion is a multi-issue dispositional opinion — a less common LSAT logical-reasoning structure but a critical real-world skill. Practice exercise: outline the four issues, identify how the court's vacate-and-remand on issues 1-2 affects whether it needs to reach issues 5-6. The "we do not reach" pattern is a constructive use of conditional reasoning that LSAT logical-reasoning rarely tests but bar-exam essay writing requires constantly.
- **Doctrinal-evolution thread:** Alice § 101 has been a doctrinal mess since 2014. The combination of (i) ChargePoint's "characterizations of the abstract idea" approach, (ii) Aatrix/Cooperative Entertainment's permission to deny motions on step two without ruling step one, and (iii) this opinion's caution about doing exactly that pre-trial — these create a complex strategic landscape for both patent owners and challengers. Worth tracking how district courts implement footnote 7's guidance.
- **Cross-stitch with Ironburg (24-2088):** Both opinions are by panels including Judge Stark, who shows in his Ironburg concurrence a willingness to elaborate doctrinal frameworks beyond the holding. The Stark-style "this is what the doctrine needs even though the case doesn't require it" approach is one judicial-writing technique worth observing.
- **The '887 patent eligibility holding is particularly worth knowing in detail** — the court's articulation of why polling-interval + conditional-transmission constraints are "specific technique" rather than "abstract result" is a model for how to frame technological-improvement claims to clear Alice step one.

## Main Opinion Summary

Judge Chen, for a panel of Chen, Cunningham, and Stark, vacates the infringement and damages judgments, vacates the § 101 ruling on the '495 patent and remands for further proceedings on Alice step two, affirms the § 101 rulings on the '887 and '371 patents (claims not directed to abstract ideas), affirms the denial of JMOL on '371 infringement, and dismisses Ollnova's prejudgment-interest appeal and ecobee's remaining Daubert appeal as moot. On verdict-form unanimity: the single combined infringement question across four patents was reversible error under *Optis Cellular Technology, LLC v. Apple Inc.*, 139 F.4th 1363 (Fed. Cir. 2025); the verdict form needed at minimum separate infringement questions per patent. On Alice § 101 instructions: where the jury was asked to resolve underlying factual disputes at step two, failing to identify the abstract idea (or instruct that the abstract idea cannot supply the inventive concept) was reversible error. On substantive § 101: the '495 patent's dual-network architecture provides sufficient inventive-concept evidence for a reasonable jury to find eligibility on remand; the '887 patent's polling-interval-and-conditional-transmission architecture and the '371 patent's COV-aggregation-with-repeat-until-acknowledgment architecture both recite specific technological solutions, escaping Alice step one. On '371 infringement: substantial evidence supports the jury's finding that ecobee's retry transmissions satisfy the "repeat" limitation even with additional information appended. AFFIRMED-IN-PART, DISMISSED-IN-PART, VACATED AND REMANDED. No costs.
