# Ereğli Demir ve Çelik Fabrikaları T.A.Ş. v. United States Int'l Trade Commission, Nos. 24-2242, 24-2243, 24-2249 (Fed. Cir. June 1, 2026)

## 1. The Issue

Three interlocking issues across a consolidated appeal from three CIT cases, all arising from the 2016 antidumping-duty (AD) order on hot-rolled steel flat products from Turkey — an order that survived even after Erdemir's co-respondent Çolakoğlu was found in 2020 to have a 0% dumping margin and was retroactively excluded from the AD order:

**(1) Sunset review (24-2249):** Whether the statutory phrase "continuation or recurrence of material injury" in the sunset-review provisions (19 U.S.C. §§ 1675(c)(1)(C), 1675a(a)(1)) requires the Commission, when Çolakoğlu's exclusion arguably rendered the surviving Turkish imports "negligible" under 19 U.S.C. § 1677(24)(A)(i), to retroactively reassess whether there ever was material injury in the first place — or whether the Commission may take its earlier (unchallenged, un-appealed) 2016 material-injury determination as a given.

**(2) Changed-circumstances review — CCR (24-2242):** Whether § 1675(b) authorizes or requires relief in a CCR that was not available in the essentially contemporaneous sunset review — such that the Trade Court erred in dismissing Erdemir's CCR-denial challenge as moot or as failing to state a claim.

**(3) Reconsideration (24-2243):** Whether the Trade Court had subject-matter jurisdiction under the residual grant of 28 U.S.C. § 1581(i) over Erdemir's challenge to the Commission's denial of reconsideration — or whether the "true nature" of that challenge was really a challenge to the 2016 material-injury determination itself, which Erdemir could have (but did not) bring under § 1581(c) within the 30-day window in 2016.

## 2. Rule Mapping

**Controlling AD framework (19 U.S.C. §§ 1673–1673h):** Commerce determines dumping (sales at less than fair value); the Commission determines material injury. If both are affirmative, Commerce issues an AD order (§ 1673e(a)). Commerce disregards de minimis dumping margins (less than 2% ad valorem, § 1673b(b)(3)); the Commission must terminate an investigation for a country whose imports are "negligible" (less than 3% of total imports, §§ 1673d(b)(1)(B), 1677(24)(A)(i)).

**Revocation mechanisms (19 U.S.C. § 1675):** Two paths — (b) **CCR**, requested by an interested party demonstrating "changed circumstances sufficient to warrant" review, with the party bearing burden of persuasion; and (c) **sunset review**, initiated automatically no later than 30 days before the fifth anniversary of an AD order, in which the AD order "shall" be revoked "unless" Commerce finds dumping likely to continue/recur AND the Commission finds material injury likely to continue/recur (§ 1675(d)(2)).

**Sunset/CCR forward-looking standard (§ 1675a(a)(1)):** In either review, "the Commission shall determine whether revocation of an order . . . would be likely to lead to continuation or recurrence of material injury within a reasonably foreseeable time . . . [by] consider[ing] the likely volume, price effect, and impact of imports of the subject merchandise on the industry if the order is revoked." The Commission must take into account "prior injury determinations" (§ 1675a(a)(1)(A)) and may cumulate country data under § 1675a(a)(7) subject to cumulation criteria.

**CIT jurisdictional framework (28 U.S.C. § 1581):** Subsection (c) grants exclusive jurisdiction over civil actions under 19 U.S.C. § 1516a (including Commission final material-injury determinations, sunset review determinations, and CCR-institution denials), subject to a 30-day filing window from Federal Register publication. Subsection (i) provides a **residual** grant of jurisdiction, expressly *not* extending to matters "reviewable by" the Trade Court under § 1516a(a) (§ 1581(i)(2)(A)).

**Residual-jurisdiction preclusion doctrine:** "Section 1581(i) jurisdiction may not be invoked when jurisdiction under another subsection of § 1581 *is or could have been available*, unless the remedy provided under that other subsection would be manifestly inadequate" (*Norcal/Crosetti Foods, Inc. v. United States*, 963 F.2d 356, 359 (Fed. Cir. 1992); *ARP Materials, Inc. v. United States*, 47 F.4th 1370, 1377 (Fed. Cir. 2022)). "Manifestly inadequate" is a high standard — pursuit must be "an exercise in futility" (*Hartford Fire Ins. Co. v. United States*, 544 F.3d 1289, 1294 (Fed. Cir. 2008)); a remedy "is not inadequate simply because [a plaintiff] failed to invoke it within the time frame it prescribes" (*Juice Farms, Inc. v. United States*, 68 F.3d 1344, 1346 (Fed. Cir. 1995)).

**"True nature" doctrine (Rimco, Wanxiang, TR International):** In deciding whether a § 1581(i) action could have been brought under another subsection, the court looks past pleading labels to "the true nature of the action" (*Rimco Inc. v. United States*, 98 F.4th 1046, 1052 (Fed. Cir. 2024)). A due-process framing does not defeat "true nature" analysis (*Wanxiang America Corp. v. United States*, 12 F.4th 1369 (Fed. Cir. 2021)); a "misapplication" framing does not either (*TR Int'l Trading Co. v. United States*, 4 F.4th 1363 (Fed. Cir. 2021)).

**Best-reading construction (post-Loper Bright):** Questions of statutory interpretation answered by determining the "best reading" of the statute (*Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, 603 U.S. 369, 400 (2024)). Statutes read in context, considering structure (*Merit Management Group, LP v. FTI Consulting, Inc.*, 583 U.S. 366, 378–82 (2018); *Transpacific Steel LLC v. United States*, 4 F.4th 1306, 1322 (Fed. Cir. 2021)).

**Statement of Administrative Action (SAA) as authoritative construction:** 19 U.S.C. § 3512(d) — the URAA SAA is "an authoritative expression by the United States concerning the interpretation and application of the [URAA] in any judicial proceeding." SAA characterizes sunset review as "inherently predictive and speculative" and "prospective in nature."

**Ripeness + protective filing:** Ripeness is assessed at the time of adjudication, considering relevant facts *then* — a contingent claim need not be justiciable at filing if a stay is available until the contingency ripens (*Blanchette v. Connecticut Gen. Ins. Corps.*, 419 U.S. 102, 140 (1974); *Kerotest Mfg. Co. v. C-O-Two Fire Equip. Co.*, 342 U.S. 180, 182–84 (1952)).

## 3. Synthesis: The "Why" and "So What"

**The Why — three holdings, one doctrinal spine:**

The consolidated opinion's holdings all rest on a single principle: **once Congress has set a statutory timing/procedural framework for challenging a determination, litigants cannot escape that framework by reframing the challenge, invoking residual jurisdiction, or seeking a backward-looking reassessment through a forward-looking review mechanism.** Finality, in the trade-law context, is a legislative choice about the balance between correctness and repose, and courts respect that balance.

**On the sunset review (Part II.A):** The panel reads "continuation or recurrence of material injury" against the future-focused structure of § 1675(c) and § 1675a. The statute "presupposes that there *has been* material injury" but does not specify where that premise comes from. Given the sunset review's plain focus on the future ("would be likely to lead," "likely," "inherently predictive and speculative" per the SAA), the "better reading" — and here the panel invokes *Loper Bright*'s best-reading command — is that the Commission "may (perhaps must)" treat the earlier authoritative final determination as a given if it has not been displaced through direct review or reconsideration. This is analogized to law-of-preclusion practice: an otherwise-settled determination of a past fact, in a later proceeding focused on the future, is treated as settling the issue. On the facts, the Commission properly excluded Çolakoğlu data from the current-review numerator (a finding on the record that "at no point did the Commission directly rely on a data set that includes Çolakoğlu's pre-exclusion imports") and reasonably cumulated Turkey with five other countries while excluding Brazil (national-security-based quota limits distinct competition).

**On the CCR (Part II.B):** The panel corrects the Trade Court's mootness framing (mootness confuses jurisdictional impossibility with merits) but affirms on Rule 12(b)(6) grounds. The move: run the CCR/sunset-review provisions in parallel and show that "the statute provides no identified difference opening an avenue for Erdemir to secure relief in a changed-circumstances review that it could not have gotten, but failed to get, in the sunset review." In fact, CCR imposes a burden of persuasion on the party seeking revocation (§ 1675(b)(3)) with no counterpart in sunset review; the SAA further reinforces finality by rejecting CCR as a vehicle for reopening based on subsequent developments. If X (CCR) provides no more than Y (sunset review) and Y failed, X fails a fortiori.

**On the reconsideration case (Part II.C):** This is the heaviest holding. Erdemir's request for reconsideration of the 2016 Commission determination was "in its true nature" a challenge to the 2016 Commission determination itself — a challenge that could have been brought in a timely § 1581(c) action back in 2016, using a **protective filing + stay** posture pending Çolakoğlu's separate dumping-challenge case. The evidence underlying Erdemir's negligibility argument was on the investigation record by the 2016 filing window's close; only the *adjudicator's reassessment* of that evidence was pending in Çolakoğlu's separate case. Under *ARP Materials* — the panel's strongest single citation — even challenges that are conditional on a later, uncertain administrative action must be timely filed to preserve the challenge. "A remedy is not inadequate simply because [a plaintiff] failed to invoke it within the time frame it prescribes" (*Juice Farms*). § 1581(i) is thus unavailable.

**The So What:**

**(1) The past-material-injury premise is settled in sunset review.** Foreign producers who missed the 30-day window to challenge an original ITC material-injury determination cannot reopen that determination through sunset review, even if a downstream development (like co-respondent exclusion) would arguably have flipped the negligibility analysis. This is a significant tightening of finality — and per footnote 7, the panel expressly reserves the question of whether **new evidence** cases might justify a different outcome, leaving a narrow doctrinal doorway for reconsideration based on previously unavailable evidence bearing on issues (like negligibility) not part of a CCR.

**(2) CCR is now formally lesser-or-equal to sunset review.** After this opinion, a party cannot use a CCR to accomplish what it failed to accomplish in a contemporaneous sunset review — the two mechanisms are functionally coextensive on the "continuation or recurrence" question, and CCR imposes an additional burden. Practical effect: strategic CCR filing (as a hedge against sunset-review failure) is now doctrinally foreclosed for the class of arguments Erdemir raised.

**(3) The "true nature" doctrine + protective-filing rule are cemented.** *ARP Materials* is elevated from a § 1581(a) protest case to a general principle applicable to § 1581(c) contexts as well. Even conditional/contingent challenges must be timely filed with a stay pending resolution of the underlying contingency. The doctrinal message to the trade bar: **"Protective filings with stays are hardly unknown."** File first, wait later.

**(4) Loper Bright is deployed for the first time in the Federal Circuit sunset-review context to command a "best reading" analysis of § 1675a.** This is a small but consequential move: post-*Loper Bright*, the Federal Circuit is doing its own statutory-interpretation work on trade-remedy statutes, not deferring to the Commission's construction. The SAA still gets its § 3512(d) authoritativeness, but the court, not the agency, is now the primary interpretive authority.

## 4. Logical Flaws & Observations

- **The "may (perhaps must)" resolution-without-choosing is the H3 signature again.** The panel repeatedly writes "may (perhaps must)" when discussing whether the Commission is required to treat the earlier material-injury determination as a given. This is exactly the *"we need not decide"* move JR flagged in LRD-002's 25-1807 — resolve the case, preserve doctrinal flexibility. **The "smallest claim that entails" is being deployed here at the doctrinal level:** the panel does the minimum to affirm and leaves the "must" question open. Doctrinal boundary preserved for future refinement.

- **The "true nature" doctrine is doing very heavy work.** Read the *Rimco*, *Wanxiang*, and *TR International* string together and you see the Federal Circuit is aggressively policing pleading-through-relabeling. A due-process challenge whose underlying complaint is really about Commerce's antidumping decision (*Wanxiang*) is treated as an antidumping decision challenge. A "misapplication" challenge whose underlying complaint is really a scope question (*TR International*) is treated as a scope challenge. **This is [[P13]] at doctrinal scale** — map by function, not by label. Erdemir's reconsideration-denial framing does not get to duck the § 1581(c) framework just because it is presented as a challenge to the reconsideration denial rather than to the underlying 2016 determination.

- **The protective-filing rule is a "smoke starts the clock" analog.** LRD-002's 25-1807 held that the inquiry-notice standard starts the DTSA statute-of-limitations clock — smoke suffices. This case's holding is the procedural mirror: once you know of a conditional basis for a claim (Çolakoğlu's challenge to Commerce's dumping determination), you cannot wait for the fire (Çolakoğlu's success) — you must protect the claim by filing within the statutory window and requesting a stay. **Both cases reward promptness over ripeness.** In substantive law (25-1807): smoke starts the SOL clock. In procedural law (this case): smoke starts the 30-day § 1581(c) window. **Same epistemic threshold, deployed at different doctrinal levels.**

- **The panel corrects the Trade Court on mootness — but affirms on 12(b)(6).** This is a **preservation of doctrinal cleanliness** move: mootness is jurisdictional impossibility; 12(b)(6) is merits failure. Confusing them (as the Trade Court did) blurs a distinction the Supreme Court has been emphasizing (*Chafin v. Chafin*; *Shapiro v. McManus*; *Steel Co.*). The Federal Circuit uses affirm-on-alternative-grounds to teach the doctrinal distinction without disturbing the outcome. This is the *ARP*-flavored move — get to the same result but through the doctrinally correct door.

- **Footnote 7 is where the future litigation lives.** "We have no occasion to address issues that may arise if reconsideration is sought, after the time for appeal has run, based on previously unavailable evidence, where the evidence bears on an issue, such as negligibility, that is material to the determination for which reconsideration is sought but is not part of a changed-circumstances review under 19 U.S.C. § 1675(b)." Translation: **if the evidence underlying your reconsideration request was actually unavailable at the time of the 2016 window, you might have a live § 1581(i) argument.** But Erdemir's ground was "readily available during the time allowed for an immediate action. The facts themselves were available — just not, yet, an adjudicator's reassessment of the facts." **The line is drawn at unavailable-evidence, not unavailable-adjudication.** Future litigants will test this line.

- **The SAA's role is quietly load-bearing.** The panel cites the SAA three times, once per holding, always at doctrinally-load-bearing moments (sunset review's prospective nature; CCR's finality function; SAA's guidance against reopening based on dumping-margin changes). The § 3512(d) authoritative-expression status is treated as robust — not just legislative history but something closer to authoritative interpretive canon. **Worth naming the SAA as a discrete construction tool for trade-law analysis** — it is neither statute nor regulation but has near-statutory interpretive weight.

- **Cumulation reasonableness is affirmed without much interrogation.** The Commission cumulated Turkey with five other countries and excluded Brazil (national-security quota). Erdemir attacked the Turkey-Çolakoğlu-data-exclusion but not (apparently forcefully) the Brazil-cumulation-exclusion. The panel affirms on a "reasonably cumulated" formulation, which is deferential substantial-evidence review. There is at least one interesting sub-question the case does not reach: **does a national-security volume quota make imports "not likely to compete" with others as a matter of law?** The panel treats this as unremarkable, but the doctrinal test in § 1675a(a)(7) is "likely to compete with each other" — a national-security cap constrains volume, not necessarily competition. Future litigants may test this.

- **Taranto is a heavyweight on jurisdictional and administrative-law doctrine, and this opinion reads like it.** Dense citation, careful boundary-drawing, doctrinally clean corrections of the lower court, and pragmatic deployment of *ARP* and *Loper Bright* as engines. Cunningham and Stark join without separate opinions — solid consensus.

## 5. Follow-ups for Mr. C

- **LSAT reasoning angle (Sufficient Assumption + statutory canon at the sunset-review level):** The panel's argument for treating the earlier material-injury determination as a given works as a Sufficient Assumption exercise. **Stimulus:** § 1675(c) requires the Commission to determine whether revocation would lead to "continuation or recurrence of material injury"; § 1675a(a) is forward-focused; § 1675(d) governs revocation prospectively; the SAA characterizes the review as "prospective in nature." **Conclusion:** the Commission may take the earlier material-injury determination as a given. **Missing premise (the plug-in):** an authoritative final determination not displaced through direct review or reconsideration is properly treated as settling the fact (a preclusion-derived sufficient assumption). Plug it in → conclusion follows. **[[Sufficient_Assumption_anchor]] applied at statutory-review scale.**

- **LSAT reasoning angle (map by function, not label — "true nature" doctrine):** The panel's move under § 1581(i) is exactly [[P13]]. Erdemir framed the challenge as a challenge to the reconsideration denial. The panel asks: **what does the challenge actually do?** It asks to undo the 2016 material-injury determination retroactively. That is § 1581(c) work regardless of the label. Practice exercise: **rewrite Erdemir's complaint** in a way that isolates a challenge that is genuinely to the reconsideration denial (not the underlying determination) — e.g., a challenge to the Commission's articulation of the "extraordinary circumstances" standard as arbitrary and capricious. Whether such a claim would survive § 1581(i) analysis is a different question, but the exercise builds the function-vs-label muscle.

- **LSAT reasoning angle (protective-filing rule as inquiry-notice's procedural mirror):** Pair this case's protective-filing rule with 25-1807's inquiry-notice rule. Both hold that **suspicion + a known conditional basis** starts the clock. In DTSA (25-1807): smoke (former employee + look-alike product) starts the SOL. In § 1581(c) (this case): a known separate challenge with a known conditional link starts the 30-day appeal window. Cross-doctrine pattern named: **"the clock starts when you can see the shape, not when you can prove the substance."** Consider promoting this to a war-chest item.

- **LSAT reasoning angle (resolution-without-choosing — third data point on H3):** The "may (perhaps must)" formulation in this opinion joins 25-1807's three deferred questions as another data point on H3 (court "we need not decide" mirrors "too simple to be right"). The panel resolves what it must, defers what it can, and preserves doctrinal flexibility. **H3 is now confirmed on at least three cases in the corpus** (25-1807's three declinations + this case's may/must reservation + footnote 7's unavailable-evidence reservation).

- **Doctrinal-evolution thread (residual jurisdiction limits):** *Norcal/Crosetti Foods* (1992) → *Juice Farms* (1995) → *International Custom Products* (2006) → *Ford Motor* (2012) → *Sunpreme* (2018) → *ARP Materials* (2022) → *Rimco* (2024) → this case (2026). The § 1581(i)-preclusion doctrine has been getting incrementally tightened for over three decades. **The 2026 addition:** applying the *ARP* protective-filing principle to the § 1581(c) context. Track for future refinement: does the doctrine now reach § 1581(a) protest contexts symmetrically?

- **Cross-stitch with LRD-002 corpus:**
  - **[[25-1807|Insulet v. EOFlow]]** — inquiry-notice starts the SOL clock; suspicion suffices for pleading (this case's protective-filing rule is the procedural analog)
  - **[[24-2088|Ironburg v. Valve]]** — hindsight-bias / skilled-searcher standard; what a party "reasonably could have" found (this case's "the facts themselves were available" language is the same conceptual move applied to statutory-window compliance)
  - **[[24-2369|Chafin v. OPM]]** — *Russello* canon; textual construction (this case's *Loper Bright* best-reading move is a modern successor)

- **Cross-stitch with [[24-1990|Jacobson v. US]] (companion case in LRD-003):** Both invoke *Ruhrgas* / *Sinochem* "no unyielding jurisdictional hierarchy." Both are unanimous affirmances of dismissals for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction. **Both are about the boundary between statutory finality choices and constitutional review.** Pairable in SR session on **"when the statutory scheme forecloses review, when it merely channels it."** Jacobson: statute forecloses review outright (§ 4208). Erdemir: statute channels review to a specific subsection with a timing rule (§ 1581(c)) and forecloses residual jurisdiction as escape hatch.

- **Doctrinal-evolution thread (Loper Bright deployment):** This is one of the earlier Federal Circuit trade-law cases to explicitly cite and apply *Loper Bright*'s best-reading command (603 U.S. 369, 400 (2024)). Track future opinions to see whether the Federal Circuit uses *Loper Bright* to unwind prior *Chevron*-flavored deference to Commerce and Commission constructions, or whether the effect is smaller (best-reading + SAA authoritativeness may produce the same results in most cases).

- **Watch thread:** Erdemir has run out of Trade Court paths — the three-way affirmance leaves only Supreme Court certiorari (unlikely to be granted on trade-remedy procedural mechanics of this sort) or waiting for the next sunset review in ~2031. The AD order on hot-rolled steel from Turkey continues in force with respect to Erdemir's imports; Çolakoğlu remains excluded.

## Main Opinion Summary

Judge Taranto, joined unanimously by Judges Cunningham and Stark, affirms three Trade Court judgments in a consolidated appeal, resolving three interlocking challenges Erdemir brought after Çolakoğlu's exclusion from the 2016 AD order. **Sunset review (24-2249):** The forward-focused statutory language of §§ 1675(c) and 1675a(a) does not require the Commission to retroactively reassess whether there ever was material injury; the Commission may (perhaps must) treat the earlier authoritative final determination as a given if it has not been displaced through direct review or reconsideration. On the merits, the Commission properly excluded Çolakoğlu data from the sunset-review analysis (the record confirms no reliance on Çolakoğlu's pre-exclusion imports in the numerator) and reasonably cumulated Turkey with five other countries while excluding Brazil (national-security-based volume quota). **CCR (24-2242):** The Trade Court erred in dismissing for mootness (which confuses jurisdictional impossibility with merits) but the dismissal is affirmed under Rule 12(b)(6). The statute provides no path for a CCR to give relief a sunset review could not — the two mechanisms are functionally coextensive on the "continuation or recurrence" question, and CCR imposes an additional burden of persuasion. **Reconsideration (24-2243):** The Trade Court correctly held it lacked § 1581(i) jurisdiction because the "true nature" of Erdemir's challenge was to the 2016 material-injury determination itself, which could have been challenged under § 1581(c) in 2016 via a protective filing + stay pending Çolakoğlu's separate case. The evidence underlying Erdemir's negligibility argument was on the record by the 2016 window's close; only the adjudicator's reassessment was pending. Under *ARP Materials* and *Juice Farms*, "a remedy is not inadequate simply because [a plaintiff] failed to invoke it within the time frame it prescribes." AFFIRMED (all three). Parties bear their own costs.
