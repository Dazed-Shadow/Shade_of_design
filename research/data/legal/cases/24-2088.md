# Ironburg Inventions Ltd. v. Valve Corporation, No. 24-2088 (Fed. Cir. June 18, 2026)

## 1. The Issue

Whether a district court correctly applied the "skilled searcher" standard of 35 U.S.C. § 315(e)(2) when it estopped Valve from raising two invalidity grounds at trial — specifically, (a) whether mere accessibility of prior art through a classification search returning thousands of references constitutes "discovery" by a skilled searcher, and (b) whether post-IPR-petition searches that used forward-and-backward citation queries (capturing later-arising references) can constitute probative evidence of pre-petition discoverability.

## 2. Rule Mapping

**Controlling statute:** 35 U.S.C. § 315(e)(2) estops an IPR petitioner that received a final written decision from later asserting in civil litigation "any ground that the petitioner raised or reasonably could have raised during . . . inter partes review."

**Governing test (Ironburg I, 64 F.4th 1274):** The patent owner bears the burden of proving that the asserted invalidity grounds were ones a "skilled searcher conducting a diligent search reasonably could have been expected to discover" at the time of the IPR petition.

**Refinements adopted today:**
- Evidence shedding light on what a skilled searcher would have discovered *only after* the IPR petition is "likely irrelevant to the skilled searcher inquiry."
- A classification search returning an unreviewably large set of references (here, 26,333) does NOT, standing alone, establish discovery; "something more" — keyword narrowing, manual review, citation-based filtering — is required.
- A § 315(e)(2) "ground" is not the prior art reference itself; the patentee must prove discoverability of the *ground*, not just the references on which it is built (citing Ingenico, 136 F.4th 1354 (Fed. Cir. 2025)).

## 3. Synthesis: The "Why" and "So What"

**The Why:** The Federal Circuit reversed because the district court treated mere classification-search inclusion as the endpoint of the skilled-searcher analysis. The 26,333 returned references for Kotkin meant a skilled searcher would not, in fact, have *found* the reference even though they could have *accessed* the classification containing it. As to the Cardinal IP evidence offered by Ironburg on remand, the court held the searches were tainted by hindsight bias from an early citation-based search (string #3) that would have pulled in later-arising references citing the disputed prior art — Mr. Hameder's later "correction" applying date filters only to a different search (string #34) came "too little, too late." A supplemental search undertaken explicitly "because there was another reference that had not yet been found" is by definition hindsight-driven and not probative.

**The So What:** This opinion meaningfully raises the evidentiary bar for IPR-estoppel proponents. Going forward, classification-search numerosity matters, and forensic searches conducted years post-petition will be scrutinized for hindsight infection from their earliest steps. Patent owners can no longer rely on the simple narrative "the reference is in a classification the petitioner searched, therefore estopped" — they must show that within the search workflow's natural cadence, the reference would have surfaced for review. For petitioners, the holding revives both the Kotkin and Willner-Koji-Raymond grounds on remand, with significant downstream impact on the $4M+ jury verdict that was ultimately built on the estoppel ruling.

## 4. Logical Flaws & Observations

- **The court explicitly leaves two doctrinal questions open:** (1) whether the skilled-searcher inquiry is a question of law (de novo) or fact (clear error), and (2) what "discover[y]" requires beyond reference-location (per the Palomar standard requiring the searcher "read, interpret, and understand" the reference). Both questions are deferred under the rationale that "we conclude that Kotkin was not discoverable . . . under any conception" — a familiar judicial move that resolves the case while preserving flexibility, but leaves district courts and the Board with continuing uncertainty.
- **The hindsight-bias analysis has a subtle internal tension.** The court holds that an early citation-based search (Greenia search #3) infected all downstream iterative searches because Greenia "then crafted keywords and selected additional classifications to guide his subsequent iterative searches." But citation-based searching is acknowledged by the court itself as a legitimate, conventional patent-search technique. The line between "conventional iterative methodology" and "hindsight-tainted methodology" depends almost entirely on whether the searcher knew the target references in advance — a fact-bound inquiry that resists clean rule-formation.
- **The Stark concurrence is doing significant doctrinal work** that is not formally part of the holding but reads like a roadmap. Stark distinguishes "findability" (locating a reference) from "discoverability" (recognizing the invalidity ground based on the reference). He suggests that where an invalidity ground is "so apparent on the face of the prior art reference that, once the reference is found, the invalidity ground is necessarily discovered," findability may collapse into discoverability. The Willner reference (cited on the face of the asserted patent) is offered as the prototypical example of findability satisfied; Kotkin is the counterexample. This two-step framework is not yet binding circuit precedent but is likely to be cited heavily in future § 315(e)(2) litigation.

## 5. Follow-ups for Mr. C

- **LSAT reasoning angle:** This opinion is a near-perfect specimen of the analytical-reasoning structure tested on the LSAT logical-reasoning section. The court (i) identifies a statutory standard, (ii) interprets a key term ("discover") narrowly, (iii) demonstrates the lower court applied an under-inclusive version of that standard, (iv) shows the only available evidence cannot satisfy the corrected standard, (v) reverses. Practice exercise: identify the formal logical structure — what kind of argument is "the only evidence offered cannot support the conclusion under any conception of the standard"? (Hint: it's a form of disjunctive elimination.)
- **Doctrinal-evolution thread:** Track Ironburg I → Ingenico → Ironburg II → next IPR-estoppel appeal. The Stark two-step framework (findability vs discoverability) is the next doctrinal frontier; whoever briefs it carefully in the next case has high leverage.
- **Bridge to other Asserted Patents reasoning:** The opinion's footnote 5 quietly does a lot of work — because Raymond and Koji are part of a single combined invalidity ground, a finding that one element is not discoverable necessarily kills the ground (Ingenico). This is a "weakest link" doctrine for invalidity combinations worth knowing in detail.

## Main Opinion Summary

Judge Hughes, writing for a panel of Chen, Hughes, and Stark, reverses the district court's IPR estoppel ruling on both invalidity grounds. As to Kotkin, the Landon IP classification search returning 26,333 references is insufficient to establish discoverability without narrowing — "something more is required for estoppel when a classification search alone returns an unreviewable number of search results." As to Willner-Koji-Raymond, the Cardinal IP search evidence is tainted by hindsight bias from an early forward-and-backward citation search that would have returned post-petition references citing the disputed prior art; Mr. Hameder's late-applied date filters only to a different search are insufficient. Because the patent owner bore the burden and the evidence cannot meet it, no remand for further fact-finding is warranted on the estoppel issue itself, and the district court must consider the invalidity of the '525 patent in light of both Kotkin and Willner-Koji-Raymond. REVERSED AND REMANDED. Costs to appellant.

## Concurring Opinion Summary

Judge Stark concurs in full but writes separately to highlight three contributions of the panel opinion to the developing skilled-searcher doctrine. First, he proposes a two-step framework for the patentee's burden under § 315(e)(2): (i) prove the prior art references were *findable* by a skilled searcher, and (ii) prove the skilled searcher reasonably would have *discovered* the invalidity ground based on those references — building on the Ingenico distinction between "grounds" and "references." Today's case decides only the findability step (Valve's appeal did not separately challenge step two). Second, he agrees with the majority that "something more" than mere accessibility is required at step one when classification searches return unreviewable numbers of references; the Kotkin example shows accessibility without findability, while Willner (cited on the face of the asserted patent) illustrates findability satisfied. Third, he acknowledges the inherent fact-specificity of the inquiry and the limited number of appeals giving the court opportunity to develop the doctrine — necessitating that much of the "something more" requirement's development be left to district courts and the Board.
