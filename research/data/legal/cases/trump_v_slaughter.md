# Trump v. Slaughter, No. 25-332 (U.S. June 29, 2026)

## 1. The Issue

Whether the Federal Trade Commission Act's for-cause removal restriction on FTC Commissioners (15 U.S.C. § 41 — Commissioners removable only "for inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office") is consistent with the separation of powers under Article II of the Constitution. Sub-question, dispositive of the case: **whether *Humphrey's Executor v. United States*, 295 U.S. 602 (1935)**, which for 90 years has permitted for-cause protection of FTC-style multimember commissions on the theory that they exercise "quasi-legislative" and "quasi-judicial" (not "purely executive") powers, **should be overruled**.

## 2. Rule Mapping

**Article II Vesting Clause + Take Care Clause:** "The executive Power shall be vested in a President of the United States of America." Art. II, § 1, cl. 1. President "shall take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed." Art. II, § 3. Together they establish, on the majority's reading, a **hierarchy** — Chief Magistrate with subordinates who "derive their offices from his appointment" and remain "subject to his superintendence." *The Federalist* No. 72, at 436 (Hamilton).

**The Decision of 1789:** During the First Congress's debate over structuring executive departments, Madison and allies successfully urged that "the power of removal" is part of "the Executive power" vested in the President, which "the Legislature has no right to diminish or modify." 1 Annals of Cong. 463. Bill establishing Department of Foreign Affairs was amended to assume (not confer) Presidential removal power. "Regular course of practice" that "liquidate[d] & settle[d]" the President's power of removal. 8 Writings of James Madison 450 (Hunt ed.). Confirmed by *Ex parte Hennen*, 13 Pet. 230, 259 (1839), and *Parsons v. United States*, 167 U.S. 324, 330 (1897).

***Myers v. United States*, 272 U.S. 52 (1926):** Chief Justice Taft's landmark opinion holding that the President has constitutional power to remove executive officers without Senate consent, notwithstanding contrary statute. "The vesting of the executive power in the President was essentially a grant of the power to execute the laws," including power to "remov[e] those for whom he can not continue to be responsible." *Id.* at 117. **Rule: President's removal power flows from Article II and cannot be modified by Congress for principal executive officers.**

***Humphrey's Executor v. United States*, 295 U.S. 602 (1935) — OVERRULED TODAY:** Distinguished *Myers* by holding that FTC Commissioners exercise "no part of the executive power," but rather "quasi-legislative" and "quasi-judicial" functions. Because these were not "purely executive" powers, Congress could impose for-cause removal restrictions. *Id.* at 624, 628. **The rule the Court today discards** as "a result in search of a rationale."

**Modern removal doctrine (developed 2010–2020):**
- *Free Enterprise Fund v. PCAOB*, 561 U.S. 477 (2010): Refused to extend *Humphrey's* to two-layered for-cause protection; struck down as "novel structure."
- *Seila Law LLC v. CFPB*, 591 U.S. 197 (2020): Refused to extend *Humphrey's* to single-director agency; described *Humphrey's* as limited to "an expert agency led by a group of principal officers removable by the President only for good cause."
- *Collins v. Yellen*, 594 U.S. 220 (2021): Continued cabining of *Humphrey's*.

**Stare decisis framework (Knick factors):** Quality of decision's reasoning; consistency with other cases; workability of rule; reliance interests. *Knick v. Township of Scott*, 588 U.S. 180, 203 (2019).

**Historical-tradition carveout (post-*Slaughter*):** Federal Reserve preserved as constitutional exception "to the extent that it follows in the distinct historical tradition of the First and Second Banks of the United States." *Seila Law*, 591 U.S. at 222, n. 8, made load-bearing today. Non-Article III courts (Tax Court, Court of Federal Claims) reserved for another day.

## 3. Synthesis: The "Why" and "So What"

**The Why.** *Slaughter* is the case that formally overrules *Humphrey's Executor* — a 91-year-old precedent that structured the modern administrative state — and thereby establishes as constitutional doctrine what the Court has been telegraphing for a decade: **principal officers exercising executive power must be removable by the President at will**. The majority's path travels three doctrinal moves, each of which is worth tracing separately because they operate at different altitudes:

- **Text + history + structure (Part II-A).** The majority reads Article II's vesting clause, in light of Convention debates and the Decision of 1789, to require a unitary executive with unbroken removal authority. Key structural principle: "unity" of the executive would be "destroyed" if it were subject to "the control and co-operation of others" (Federalist No. 70). Executive officers are "assistants or deputies" who "derive their offices from [the President's] appointment" and are subject to his "superintendence." *Federalist No. 72*. Because officers exercise the President's power (not their own), they must be removable by him. The Decision of 1789 is treated as a "liquidation" (Madison's word) of the Constitution's meaning — a founding-era consensus that the majority reads as binding. *Cf.* *Chiafalo v. Washington*, 591 U.S. 578 (2020).

- **Precedent (Part II-B): *Myers* right, *Humphrey's* wrong.** The majority rehabilitates *Myers* (previously narrowed as concerning only Senate-consent restrictions) as controlling authority for a broad rule of at-will removal. *Humphrey's* is characterized as a "result in search of a rationale" whose "quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial" framework "has not withstood the test of time." Justice Scalia's dissent in *Morrison v. Olson*, 487 U.S. 654, 725 (1988) — the "dustbin of repudiated constitutional principles" line — is invoked as vindicated. *Humphrey's* is now "far out of step with our cases, which have all but limited it to its facts."

- **Stare decisis (Part II-B(2)(c)): every *Knick* factor cuts against retention.** Quality of reasoning: *Humphrey's* transformed executive powers into "quasi" functions "only by blind feats of definition" (quoting Peter Strauss). Consistency: *Humphrey's* has been retreated from at every turn. Workability: no one knows how to apply *Humphrey's* — lower courts have splintered on whether it protects all multimember agencies, only bipartisan ones, only those matching the 1935 FTC exactly, etc. Reliance: the majority acknowledges Congress relied on *Humphrey's* to create dozens of independent agencies but characterizes this as "the reliance interests of the American people . . . in the preservation of our constitutionally promised liberties" — reframing reliance to run in favor of overruling.

**The historical-tradition carveout is deliberately narrow.** The majority takes care in Part III-B to preserve the Federal Reserve, activated in the companion case *Trump v. Cook*, as a constitutional exception based on its lineage from the First and Second Banks of the United States. Non-Article III courts (Tax Court, Court of Federal Claims) are also reserved. Everything else — every other multimember independent commission with for-cause protection (SEC, NLRB, MSPB, NRC, FERC, CPSC, FCC, and dozens more) — is now presumptively subject to Presidential at-will removal.

**Gorsuch's concurrence carries the deeper structural warning.** Congress delegated so much legislative and judicial power to "independent" agencies in reliance on *Humphrey's*'s independence guarantee. Now that the independence is gone, the President inherits vast legislative and judicial functions along with executive control. Gorsuch flags this as ratchet-effect: authorities Congress once delegated by simple majority "may now require a veto-proof supermajority to retrieve." His prescription: revive nondelegation, sharpen the major questions doctrine, apply *Jarkesy*/Seventh Amendment discipline to in-house adjudications. "It is time to return, all the way, to the Constitution."

**Sotomayor's dissent (joined by Kagan and Jackson) is the historical + stare decisis counterpunch.** The dissent's structural argument: (a) text of Article II says nothing about removal power; (b) Framers explicitly did not give the President a removal power the English Crown lacked; (c) the Decision of 1789 addressed *Senate consent* to removals (rejected), not *fixed-term protections* (unresolved); (d) 140 years of continuous practice — beginning with the ICC (1887), Board of General Appraisers (1890), Federal Reserve Board (1913), FTC (1914), and continuing to dozens of modern agencies — shows the political branches have jointly settled the constitutional question in favor of Congressional power to impose modest removal limits. On stare decisis: *Humphrey's* generated 91 years of massive reliance; the overruling is "a profound bait and switch on a coequal branch."

**The So What.** Four propositions land as usable precedent:

1. ***Humphrey's Executor* is overruled.** The Court is explicit: "If anything more is left of *Humphrey's*, we overrule it." Multimember independent commissions with for-cause protection are presumptively unconstitutional. Any current commissioner subject to for-cause protection can be removed at will by the President; any pending litigation seeking to enforce such protections will lose on the merits.

2. **The unitary executive theory is now doctrine, not aspiration.** All powers within the "heartland of executive power" (rulemaking against private parties, enforcement, civil suits, in-house adjudications regulating private conduct) must be exercised by officers removable by the President at will. This is a formalist Article II holding — "no ifs, ands, or quasis about it" (Sotomayor dissent quoting the majority).

3. **The historical-tradition carveout is real but narrow.** Federal Reserve preserved (via *Cook*); non-Article III courts reserved; congressionally-chartered entities without executive/sovereign power (Boy Scouts, Georgetown University) unaffected. Any other agency claiming carveout must show founding-era pedigree analogous to central banking — a very high bar the majority does not explicitly define. Expect litigation testing the edges (FDIC? OCC? SEC? PCAOB post-*Free Enterprise Fund*?).

4. **Reliance-interests analysis for stare decisis is now malleable.** The majority reframes Congress's reliance on *Humphrey's* as *illegitimate* reliance ("no branch may rely on adverse possession to claim power the Constitution vests elsewhere") and elevates a competing category — "reliance interests of the American people . . . in the preservation of our constitutionally promised liberties" — as controlling. This move is portable: whenever a majority believes a longstanding precedent is constitutionally wrong, the reliance analysis can be recast in favor of overruling.

Downstream, cascading: expect executive orders removing commissioners across dozens of agencies; legal challenges dismissed on the merits under *Slaughter*; Congressional consideration of statutory workarounds (though Gorsuch's ratchet-effect note suggests these are unlikely to survive Presidential veto); litigation testing whether inferior officers and civil-service employees fall within *Slaughter*'s logic (majority does not resolve; Sotomayor dissent flags as open); and litigation over which agencies (if any) can claim the Federal Reserve's historical-tradition carveout.

## 4. Logical Flaws & Observations

- **The move that is doing the most work: reframing reliance.** *Slaughter*'s stare decisis analysis has to overcome 91 years of legislative and Executive Branch reliance on *Humphrey's*. The majority does this by (a) characterizing Congress's reliance as illegitimate ("adverse possession") and (b) inventing a competing reliance category ("the American people . . . in the preservation of our constitutionally promised liberties"). Both moves are contestable. Congress relied on *Humphrey's* because *this Court* told Congress to. And the "American people's" reliance is asserted, not demonstrated — no polling, no historical evidence, no showing that the public understood their liberties to require unitary Presidential control of the FTC. **This is the load-bearing rhetorical move**, and it's worth naming as such because the same move will be available in future stare-decisis overrulings.

- **Gorsuch's concurrence is the most doctrinally consequential opinion in the case.** The majority overrules *Humphrey's*. Gorsuch says (correctly) that this is only the first step — the harder problem is that Congress delegated so much legislative and judicial power to "independent" agencies. Now the President inherits all of it. Gorsuch's answer is to revive nondelegation, major questions, and Article III/Seventh Amendment doctrine to strip agencies of the *powers* Congress gave them. **If the concurrence's program becomes majority in future terms, the administrative state as we know it does not survive.** This is a signal worth naming — Gorsuch is explicit that "it is time to return, all the way, to the Constitution."

- **The "quasi" terminology critique cuts both ways.** The majority mocks *Humphrey's* "retreat to the qualifying 'quasi'" (Justice Jackson's line from *FTC v. Ruberoid Co.*, 343 U.S. 470, 487–488 (1952)). But administrative law has long treated agencies as exercising functionally distinct powers even where formally executive — that's the whole edifice of *Chevron*, *Skidmore*, APA § 706 review, agency adjudication under *Wong Yang Sung* and *Butz v. Economou*, etc. Overruling *Humphrey's* does not resolve the underlying doctrinal question of how to categorize hybrid agency functions; it just declares by fiat that all such functions are "executive." That declaration will produce line-drawing problems the majority doesn't address.

- **The historical-tradition carveout is untheorized.** The majority preserves the Federal Reserve as an exception "sanctioned by history." Sotomayor's dissent presses the obvious question: what other agencies qualify? What is the test? The majority declines to say. The concurrence and dissents in *Cook* highlight that this is a new (or newly-explicit) doctrinal category — "general rule + historical-tradition carveout" — that will require future development. Barrett's dissent in *Cook* names it precisely: "history [used] to support both a categorical rule and a carveout" is an unstable structure.

- **Party presentation at the certiorari-before-judgment stage.** *Slaughter* is a certiorari-before-judgment case, granted after a District Court summary judgment and a D.C. Circuit stay denial. That's a very thin record for overruling a 91-year-old precedent. The majority relies heavily on scholarly work, prior dissents, and its own recent removal cases. Alito's dissent in *Cook* names the concern: emergency-docket and expedited-cert cases risk deciding too much on too little.

- **The Decision of 1789 as constitutional-interpretation tool.** The majority reads the Decision of 1789 as a Madisonian "liquidation" of the Constitution's meaning — founding-era practice becomes controlling constitutional interpretation. Sotomayor's dissent contests both the historical record (the Decision addressed Senate consent, not tenure protection) and the interpretive theory (post-ratification practice can inform but not fix constitutional meaning). This methodological dispute — how much weight founding-era practice deserves — recurs across constitutional law. *Slaughter* is a strong pro-liquidation opinion; watch for it to shape future methodological debates.

## 5. Follow-ups for Mr. C

- **Doctrinal-scaffold angle (for JR's returning-paralegal register):** The core stare-decisis move in *Slaughter* is a **four-factor overruling framework** (Knick): quality, consistency, workability, reliance. Notable that the majority runs all four and finds each cuts against *Humphrey's* — this is the *maximalist* overruling posture. Compare to overrulings that rely on fewer factors (*Dobbs* emphasized quality + workability; *Loper Bright* emphasized quality + reliance-recast). **The lesson worth naming:** the Knick framework is malleable in application; different overrulings emphasize different factors, and reliance-reframing has become a portable technique.

- **Weight-of-words angle:** "Quasi" is doing enormous rhetorical work in this case — mocked by the majority (Jackson's "counterpane to conceal a disordered bed" line), defended by the dissent as a "well-established term-of-art" with pedigree from Madison through Taft. The word carries the entire administrative-state settlement: whether agency functions can be *partially* legislative or judicial, or must be *categorically* executive. **When the same word is used mockingly by one opinion and treated as load-bearing by another, that word is the case.** Same pattern as "reasonable" in tort law (mocked as vacuous, defended as necessary) and "just and equitable" in equity (same).

- **Structural analogy (rigid statutes, flexible analysis — inverted):** *Slaughter* is the *inverse* of your rigid-statute-flexible-analysis observation. Here the *Constitution* is treated as rigid (Article II vesting = unitary executive removal) and the *analysis* of what falls within its scope is treated as expansive (all "executive" power). Compare to *Cook*, decided the same day, which does the opposite — flexible constitutional analysis (historical-tradition carveout) reading a rigid-looking statute ("for cause" as substantial-threshold requirement). **The two cases together illustrate that "rigid vs. flexible" is not a property of the text; it's a property of the interpretive move.**

- **The historical-tradition safe harbor as a doctrine to watch.** *Slaughter* announces a general rule; *Cook* activates the Federal Reserve carveout. This is a **general-rule + historical-tradition-carveout** doctrinal shape, and it is likely to recur wherever the Court's structural theory conflicts with longstanding practice. Analogous shape: *Rahimi*'s "consistent with the principles that underpin the historical tradition" test in Second Amendment law. Same doctrinal move: rigid modern rule + flexible historical safe harbor. **Worth naming as a category** because it applies wherever originalism meets long-practice-based reliance.

- **Cross-stitch with prior corpus.** *Slaughter* is the top of the removal-law tree. All future work on Article II, independent agencies, civil-service protection, MSPB status, adjudicative-body independence, and nondelegation-adjacent doctrine traces back here. Pair with [[trump_v_cook|*Trump v. Cook*]] as the immediate companion. **Recommend an SR Sharpens synthesis pass** treating *Slaughter* + *Cook* as a paired doctrinal unit — the two cases are inseparable and should be studied together, not in isolation.

## Main Opinion Summary

Chief Justice Roberts, writing for a six-Justice majority (Alito, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, Barrett joining in full; Thomas joining all but Part III-B), reverses the D.C. District Court's summary judgment restoring FTC Commissioner Rebecca Slaughter to her position and holds that the FTC Commissioners' for-cause removal protection (15 U.S.C. § 41) is unconstitutional under Article II of the Constitution. President Trump had fired Slaughter and fellow Democratic Commissioner Alvaro Bedoya without asserting statutory cause, citing only Article II authority; the District Court declared the removals ultra vires under *Humphrey's Executor v. United States*, 295 U.S. 602 (1935), and the D.C. Circuit denied a stay. **The Court holds:** (1) Article II's Vesting Clause and Take Care Clause vest all executive power in the President, requiring subordinate officers who exercise executive power to be removable by him at will. Text, Founding-era history, the Decision of 1789, and *Myers v. United States*, 272 U.S. 52 (1926), all support this rule. (2) *Humphrey's Executor* is overruled. Its "quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial" framework is "a result in search of a rationale" that fails every stare decisis factor (quality, consistency, workability, reliance). Justice Scalia's characterization of *Humphrey's* as belonging in the "dustbin of repudiated constitutional principles" (*Morrison v. Olson* dissent) is vindicated. (3) The FTC exercises quintessentially executive power — rulemaking, enforcement, in-house adjudication, civil suits — and its Commissioners must therefore be removable by the President at will. (4) A **historical-tradition carveout** preserves the Federal Reserve (via *Trump v. Cook*, decided the same day) as an exception "sanctioned by history" based on its lineage from the First and Second Banks of the United States. Non-Article III courts (Tax Court, Court of Federal Claims) are reserved for another day. Justice Gorsuch's concurrence emphasizes that overruling *Humphrey's* is only the first step — Congress delegated vast legislative and judicial powers to now-formerly-independent agencies, and the Court should revive nondelegation, major questions, and Article III/Seventh Amendment doctrine to restore those powers to Congress and the courts. Justice Sotomayor's dissent (joined by Kagan and Jackson) argues the majority ignores 91 years of precedent, 140 years of continuous political practice, and massive congressional reliance interests, elevating a rigid theory of unitary executive control over the Constitution's actual text and the settled understanding of coequal branches. **Judgment REVERSED and REMANDED.** Decided companion to [[trump_v_cook|Trump v. Cook]] the same day.
