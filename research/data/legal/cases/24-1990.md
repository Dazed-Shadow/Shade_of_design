# Jacobson v. United States, No. 24-1990 (Fed. Cir. June 18, 2026)

## 1. The Issue

Two interlocking issues, both jurisdictional: (1) Whether the Court of Federal Claims (CFC), when confronted with a Tucker Act claim based on a statute that contains an explicit non-reviewability provision (FIAFEA, 12 U.S.C. § 4208), is nonetheless required by *Fisher v. United States*, 402 F.3d 1167 (Fed. Cir. 2005), to first determine whether the underlying statute is "money-mandating" before dismissing for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction; and (2) Whether such a statute, when it explicitly bars judicial review of the agency action, can nonetheless supply Tucker Act jurisdiction for a Due Process challenge to *that very bar*.

## 2. Rule Mapping

**Controlling non-reviewability provision (FIAFEA § 4208):** 12 U.S.C. § 4208 — "Notwithstanding any other law, no court shall have jurisdiction over any claim based on any action taken by the Attorney General or any refusal to take action under this subchapter, except for failure to provide notification under section 4206 of this title." The statute vests the Attorney General with sole authority to determine whether a whistleblower declaration meets validity requirements (§§ 4204(c), 4205(d)(1)) and to determine whether a settlement is based on such a declaration for revenue-sharing purposes.

**Presumption of judicial review + clear/convincing rebuttal:** A "strong presumption" in favor of judicial review governs interpretation of statutes that may limit or preclude review (*Cuozzo Speed Techs., LLC v. Lee*, 579 U.S. 261, 273 (2016), quoting *Mach Mining, LLC v. E.E.O.C.*, 575 U.S. 480, 486 (2015)). The presumption is overcome only by "clear and convincing" indications that Congress intended to bar review (*Block v. Cmty. Nutrition Inst.*, 467 U.S. 340, 349–50 (1984)). *Cuozzo* held that a "final and non-appealable" formulation met the standard.

**Tucker-Act money-mandating threshold (Fisher):** When a Tucker Act claim is based on a Constitutional provision, statute, or regulation, "the trial court at the outset shall determine . . . whether the Constitutional provision, statute, or regulation is one that is money-mandating" (*Fisher*, 402 F.3d at 1173). The rule addresses Tucker Act cases where jurisdiction "blends with the merits" — not cases where an explicit statutory provision strips jurisdiction outright.

**No unyielding jurisdictional hierarchy:** Courts are not required to address jurisdictional issues in any given order (*Ruhrgas AG v. Marathon Oil Co.*, 526 U.S. 574, 578 (1999)); a court may "choose among threshold grounds for denying audience to a case on the merits" (*Sinochem Int'l Co. v. Malaysia Int'l Shipping Corp.*, 549 U.S. 422, 431 (2007), quoting *Ruhrgas*). "[J]urisdiction is vital only if the court proposes to issue a judgment on the merits" (*Sinochem*, 549 U.S. at 431).

**Tucker Act + Due Process gateway (illegal exaction):** "[T]here is no jurisdiction under the Tucker Act over a Due Process claim unless [the claim] constitutes an illegal exaction," which exists "only if money has been 'improperly exacted or retained' by the government" (*Casa de Cambio Comdiv S.A., de C.V. v. United States*, 291 F.3d 1356, 1363 (Fed. Cir. 2002)).

## 3. Synthesis: The "Why" and "So What"

**The Why:** The panel holds first that FIAFEA § 4208 clears the *Cuozzo* clear-and-convincing bar: the "notwithstanding any other law" preface + "no court shall have jurisdiction" + a single narrow enumerated exception (§ 4206 notification failure, not invoked here) leave Congress's intent to preclude review unmistakable. Given that a statute has explicitly stripped the CFC's ability to reach the merits, requiring the CFC to first perform a *Fisher* money-mandating analysis would be "the fruitless exercise of considering whether FIAFEA is money-mandating, only to dismiss her claim for lack of jurisdiction pursuant to section 4208." *Fisher*'s threshold-sequencing rule targeted the doctrinal muddle in Tucker Act cases where jurisdiction and merits intertwine — it does not command a wasted-motion ritual when Congress has independently closed the courthouse door. *Ruhrgas* and *Sinochem* buttress this: courts may pick threshold grounds; there is no jurisdictional hierarchy that forces one to be reached before another.

On the second question, Jacobson attempted a bootstrap — arguing that even if § 4208 bars review of the Attorney General's action, the CFC nonetheless has Tucker Act jurisdiction to hear a Due Process challenge *to § 4208 itself*. The panel treats this as identifying no authority: Tucker Act Due Process jurisdiction requires an illegal-exaction pleading (*Casa de Cambio*), and Jacobson did not plead illegal exaction. Her Due Process theory, absent that gateway, cannot resurrect what § 4208 forecloses.

**The So What:** Three propositions land as usable precedent. **(1) FIAFEA § 4208 is settled as jurisdiction-stripping** for future whistleblower declarant appeals — the Attorney General's determinations on declaration validity and settlement-basis attribution are unreviewable in the CFC. **(2) *Fisher*'s money-mandating-first sequencing rule is now expressly limited to Tucker Act cases without an independent jurisdiction-stripping statute** — a doctrinal boundary that had been implicit but is now on the record. This constrains future litigants who might have hoped to force a merits-adjacent analysis as a step-through to review. **(3) Due Process challenges to non-reviewability statutes, brought in the CFC under the Tucker Act, are foreclosed absent an illegal-exaction theory** — the doctrinal box is small and Jacobson didn't fit in it.

Downstream: whistleblowers under FIAFEA whose declarations are rejected by the Attorney General have no CFC path unless they can plausibly allege (a) failure of § 4206 notification, or (b) illegal exaction (money improperly retained). Congress owns this outcome by design: FIAFEA committed these judgments to the AG's sole discretion.

## 4. Logical Flaws & Observations

- **The "clear-and-convincing" standard is doing quiet work.** *Cuozzo* held that "final and non-appealable" met the bar. FIAFEA's § 4208 is arguably stronger — "no court shall have jurisdiction" + a single enumerated exception. The panel does not linger on the comparison, but the language-strength gap between the two statutes is worth noting for future statutes falling in the gray zone (e.g., statutes that use "shall be final" without more, or that use commit-to-agency-discretion language without express jurisdictional bar).

- **The panel resolves without deciding *Fisher*'s scope generally.** The holding is narrow: *Fisher* does not require money-mandating-first analysis when an independent jurisdiction-stripping statute is in play. The panel does not say *Fisher* is limited to that circumstance in every direction — it just says *Fisher* did not "involve a statute that strips the jurisdiction of the Court of Federal Claims." This is a classic **resolution-without-choosing** move: solve this case, leave the doctrinal boundary flexible for future refinement.

- **The Due Process-as-illegal-exaction gateway is very narrow.** *Casa de Cambio* requires money "improperly exacted or retained" by the government. FIAFEA is a share-in-recovery statute, not an exaction — the government took money from Wells Fargo, not from Jacobson. There is essentially no plausible illegal-exaction pleading available for a rejected FIAFEA declarant. The doctrinal ceiling on Tucker Act Due Process claims may effectively immunize non-reviewability statutes from constitutional attack in the CFC forum. Whether that's a feature (Congress's finality choice respected) or a bug (constitutional doubt about non-reviewability provisions never gets adjudicated) is a live question the panel does not engage with.

- **The Attorney General's three grounds for rejecting Jacobson's declaration are aggressive.** Ground (2) — that Jacobson "admitted to participating in the fraudulent conduct she alleged" — is a serious characterization applied to a mortgage loan officer working at Wells Fargo during the origination years, and Jacobson denies it in her complaint. Ground (3) — the "prior public disclosure" bar — is a familiar False Claims Act analog and typically requires the "original source" analysis, which the AG's rejection does not appear to have engaged in. But because § 4208 forecloses review, none of these determinations get tested — even ones that might be doctrinally shaky. The finality choice absorbs the merits.

- **Newman continues to be absent from panels.** Reyna, Wallach, Hughes — a solid three-judge panel but not a heavyweight one for jurisdictional-doctrine cases. The opinion's tight construction and clean citation-density suggest efficient consensus rather than deep panel deliberation.

## 5. Follow-ups for Mr. C

- **LSAT reasoning angle (statutory construction — presumption + rebuttal):** The *Cuozzo* / *Block* structure — strong presumption of judicial review, rebuttable only by clear-and-convincing indication — is a textbook **rebuttable presumption** structure familiar from LSAT logical reasoning. Practice angle: identify which statutory language crosses the clear-and-convincing threshold and which doesn't. "Shall be final" (probably), "notwithstanding any other law, no court shall have jurisdiction" (yes, per this case), "committed to agency discretion" (contestable). This is a **calibration-of-signal** exercise — how strong a signal does the language need to send to overcome the presumption?

- **LSAT reasoning angle (Sinochem / Ruhrgas — anti-hierarchy):** The Supreme Court's "no unyielding jurisdictional hierarchy" principle is doctrinally interesting because it inverts the natural intuition that jurisdictional questions are foundational and must precede everything else. *Sinochem*'s move — that a court may choose which threshold to reach for "denying audience" — is efficiency-driven anti-formalism. Worth naming as a discrete doctrine because it comes up in surprising places.

- **LSAT reasoning angle (Fisher-distinguishing as I-lane move):** The panel's move is textbook Lane I ("Identify") work — recognizing the *shape* of *Fisher*'s rule (a sequencing requirement for cases where jurisdiction and merits blend), then observing that *this* case has a different shape (jurisdiction independently stripped), and concluding *Fisher* doesn't reach here. This is **map by function, not by label** ([[P13]]) at the doctrinal level: the label "*Fisher* applies to Tucker Act claims" does not tell you when *Fisher* actually operates.

- **Doctrinal-evolution thread:** *Block v. Cmty. Nutrition Inst.* (1984) → *Cuozzo* (2016) → this case. The presumption-of-review doctrine has been getting incrementally sharpened. Track the language patterns Congress uses when it wants preclusion — the drafting guidance emerging from these cases is now robust enough that future statutes explicitly barring review will be drafted with *Cuozzo* and § 4208 as templates.

- **Cross-stitch with prior corpus:** This case shares the "no unyielding jurisdictional hierarchy" citation with [[24-2242|Erdemir v. ITC]], which deploys the same *Ruhrgas*/*Sinochem* principle to a different end (§ 1581(i) residual jurisdiction limits). Same doctrinal move, opposite structural outcome — worth pairing in an SR session on **when jurisdiction-sequencing rules bind and when they release.**

- **Watch thread:** No downstream — this is likely the end of Jacobson's litigation. But the FIAFEA-related whistleblower jurisprudence in the Federal Circuit is thin; this opinion will be a cornerstone citation for future rejected-declarant appeals.

## Main Opinion Summary

Judge Reyna, joined unanimously by Judges Wallach and Hughes, affirms the CFC's dismissal of Elizabeth Jacobson's complaint for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction. Jacobson filed a FIAFEA whistleblower declaration in 2012 alleging Wells Fargo's fraudulent origination of "stated income" mortgage loans; the DOJ in 2018 rejected the declaration on three grounds (no new factual element, admitted participation in the alleged conduct, prior public disclosure); six months later the government settled with Wells Fargo for $2 billion on related allegations. Jacobson sued in the CFC, arguing the CFC was required by *Fisher* to first determine whether FIAFEA is money-mandating before addressing the jurisdictional bar. **The panel holds:** (1) § 4208's language — "no court shall have jurisdiction over any claim based on any action taken by the Attorney General," subject to a single narrow exception for § 4206 notification failure — provides the *Cuozzo* "clear and convincing" indication of congressional intent to bar review. (2) *Fisher*'s money-mandating-first requirement addresses Tucker Act cases where jurisdiction blends with merits — it does not compel the "fruitless exercise" of a money-mandating analysis when Congress has independently stripped jurisdiction; the Supreme Court's *Ruhrgas* and *Sinochem* decisions confirm there is no unyielding jurisdictional hierarchy. (3) Jacobson's Due Process challenge to § 4208 itself does not survive because Tucker Act Due Process jurisdiction requires an illegal-exaction pleading, which she did not present. AFFIRMED. Costs against Jacobson.
