# AstraZeneca AB v. Mylan Pharmaceuticals Inc., No. 21-1729 (Fed. Cir. Dec. 8, 2021)

## 1. The Issue

Two issues on appeal from a Hatch-Waxman ANDA suit over generic Symbicort® pMDI (a pressurized metered-dose inhaler for asthma/COPD): (1) **Claim construction** — whether the claim term "0.001%" — the recited concentration of the suspension-stabilizer excipient PVP K25 — has its ordinary scientific meaning under the significant-figure convention (encompassing 0.0005% to 0.0014% w/w, per standard rounding rules and one significant figure) as the district court held, OR whether the specification's stability data and prosecution history's "criticality" arguments narrow the term to "that precise number, with only minor variations" (i.e., 0.00095% to 0.00104%) as Mylan proposed. (2) **Nonobviousness** — whether the district court's factual finding that the prior art (Rogueda control formulations 3 and 9) "taught away" from the claimed invention was clearly erroneous, given expert testimony that a skilled artisan would view Rogueda's poor adhesion and particle-size results as making the control formulations "unsuitable" for further experimentation.

## 2. Rule Mapping

**Claim construction framework (Phillips):** Claim terms are given their "ordinary and customary meaning" as understood by a person of ordinary skill in the art at the time of invention, read in context of the entire patent — including the specification and prosecution history (*Phillips v. AWH Corp.*, 415 F.3d 1303, 1312–13, 1315, 1317 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (en banc)). The specification is "the single best guide to the meaning of a disputed term" (id. at 1315). The ordinary meaning is not the meaning in the abstract but the meaning to the ordinary artisan after reading the entire patent (*Eon Corp. IP Holdings v. Silver Spring Networks*, 815 F.3d 1314, 1320 (Fed. Cir. 2016)).

**"Knew-how-to-express" canon (Takeda):** When the intrinsic record demonstrates that inventors knew how to use approximation language (e.g., "about") in some claim terms but chose not to use it in the disputed term, this supports a narrower construction (*Takeda Pharm. Co. v. Zydus Pharms. USA, Inc.*, 743 F.3d 1359, 1365 (Fed. Cir. 2014)).

**Canceled-claims-as-probative-evidence (PSN Illinois):** "During prosecution, an applicant may have cancelled pending claims but not amended the specification to delete disclosure relevant only to the cancelled claims. In such cases, unasserted or cancelled claims may provide 'probative evidence' that an embodiment is not within the scope of an asserted claim" (*PSN Ill., LLC v. Ivoclar Vivadent, Inc.*, 525 F.3d 1159, 1166 (Fed. Cir. 2008)).

**Prosecution-history-informs-scope (Fenner):** "Any explanation, elaboration, or qualification presented by the inventor during patent examination is relevant, for the role of claim construction is to 'capture the scope of the actual invention' that is disclosed, described, and patented" — even absent a clear-and-unmistakable disavowal (*Fenner Invs., Ltd. v. Cellco P'ship*, 778 F.3d 1320, 1323 (Fed. Cir. 2015)).

**Standard of review for claim construction:** De novo where the construction is based only on intrinsic evidence (*Teva Pharms. USA, Inc. v. Sandoz, Inc.*, 574 U.S. 318, 331 (2015)).

**Teaching-away doctrine:** A prior art reference teaches away if a skilled artisan "upon reading the reference, would be discouraged from following the path set out in the reference, or would be led in a direction divergent from the path that was taken" in the claim (*Meiresonne v. Google, Inc.*, 849 F.3d 1379, 1382 (Fed. Cir. 2017), quoting *Galderma Lab'ys, L.P. v. Tolmar, Inc.*, 737 F.3d 731, 738 (Fed. Cir. 2013)). A reference that "merely expresses a general preference for an alternative invention but does not criticize, discredit, or otherwise discourage investigation" does not teach away (*Galderma*, 737 F.3d at 738). But "implicit disparagement" — via data a skilled artisan would read as showing unsuitability — can qualify.

**Obviousness standard of review:** Legal determinations de novo; factual findings for clear error (*Merck Sharp & Dohme Corp. v. Hospira Inc.*, 874 F.3d 724, 728 (Fed. Cir. 2017)). What the prior art teaches, motivation to combine, and whether a reference teaches away are all questions of fact (*Meiresonne*, 849 F.3d at 1382).

**Significant-figure ordinary meaning (Taranto dissent):** "Standard scientific convention" gives a well-defined interval for a stated number (*Viskase Corp. v. American Nat'l Can Co.*, 261 F.3d 1316, 1320 (Fed. Cir. 2001); *Valeant Pharms. Int'l Inc. v. Mylan Pharms. Inc.*, 955 F.3d 25, 34 (Fed. Cir. 2020); *U.S. Philips Corp. v. Iwasaki Elec. Co.*, 505 F.3d 1371, 1377–78 (Fed. Cir. 2007)). A single-significant-figure number like "0.001%" thus means the interval 0.0005% to 0.0014% under rounding rules.

## 3. Synthesis: The "Why" and "So What"

**The Why (majority — Stoll, joined by Hughes):**

The majority reads "0.001%" narrowly by triangulating three intrinsic-record signals. **First,** the specification does not just recite the number — it repeatedly and specifically distinguishes formulations with **0.001% w/w PVP** from formulations with even slightly different concentrations (0.0005%, 0.01%, 0.03%, 0.05%), touting 0.001% as producing "the most stable" formulation. The stability data at Figures 3 and 5, testing PVP concentrations at 0.0001%, 0.0005%, 0.001%, 0.01%, 0.03%, and 0.05%, shows differentiation at the fourth decimal place — meaning the specification treats fourth-decimal-place variations as materially significant to stability. **Second,** the prosecution history repeatedly emphasized the "criticality" of 0.001% w/w PVP compared to "slightly more or less." The inventors originally claimed PVP ranges (e.g., "from about 0.0005 to about 0.05 %w/w"), then narrowed multiple times in response to Examiner obviousness rejections, each time emphasizing 0.001% was critical to stability — and each time dropping the "about" qualifier when narrowing to 0.001%. **Third,** the *Takeda* "knew-how-to-express" canon applies: inventors used "about" in some claim contexts but not in the asserted claims — a deliberate choice supporting narrow construction. Combined with *PSN Illinois*'s canceled-claims doctrine (inventors had claims covering 0.0005% and canceled them), the intrinsic record supports the "minor variations" construction, which the majority operationalizes as 0.00095% to 0.00104% (i.e., a 5% variation around 0.001%, not the 50% variation that AstraZeneca's construction would permit).

On **teaching away**, the majority (unanimously with Taranto) affirms the district court. Even without explicit disparagement, Rogueda's data shows Controls 3 and 9 having drastic adhesion problems and unsuitable particle sizes for inhalation; expert testimony (Dr. Young) established that a skilled artisan would read this data as **implicit disparagement** — the controls "clearly don't work" and would not be used as a basis for further experimentation. Whether a reference teaches away is a viewpoint-of-skilled-artisan question, and a court may find implicit disparagement via expert testimony even where the reference itself contains no explicit disparagement language.

**The Why (dissent-in-part — Taranto):**

Taranto's dissent is doctrinally tight and worth mapping in full. His argument runs on ordinary-meaning discipline:

1. **Significant-figure convention IS the ordinary meaning.** Prior Federal Circuit precedent (*Viskase*, *Valeant*, *U.S. Philips*) treats this as "standard scientific convention." The district court so held, and the majority does not actually dispute it — the majority just concludes the intrinsic record displaces it.
2. **"Minor variations" fails as a construction on its face.** It adds uncertainty rather than clarifying claim scope, working against the "core purpose of claim construction, which is to *clarify* claim scope" (*U.S. Surgical Corp. v. Ethicon, Inc.*, 103 F.3d 1554, 1568 (Fed. Cir. 1997); *Arlington Indus., Inc. v. Bridgeport Fittings, Inc.*, 759 F.3d 1333, 1338 (Fed. Cir. 2014)). "Minor variations" effectively reinstates the "about" language AstraZeneca dropped — but with even less definition than "about" would have.
3. **The majority's "0.0010%" equivalence rewrites the claim.** Adding a significant figure changes the term. The specification and prosecution history uniformly used one significant figure when describing PVP concentrations — never two. This case is "critically different from *Viskase*" (261 F.3d 1316), where the patentees themselves used an extra significant figure in prosecution history to distinguish prior art. Here, they did not.
4. **The overlap between "0.001%" and "0.0005%" significant-figure intervals does NOT negate the distinction.** Overlap between two ranges is common and does not imply the ranges are the same. Two claims requiring "3 to 7" and "1 to 4" of a component are distinct in coverage despite overlap. The specification's differentiation between 0.001% and 0.0005% test results does not require rewriting the ordinary meaning to eliminate overlap.
5. **Even IF overlap justified some narrowing, the maximum justified move is overlap-exclusion (0.00055% to 0.0014%), not a full rewrite to 0.0010%.** And Mylan's ANDA product falls within even that narrower range — so the court needed only to reach the overlap-exclusion question if it reached anything (*Vivid Techs., Inc. v. American Science & Eng'g, Inc.*, 200 F.3d 795, 803 (Fed. Cir. 1999) — construe "only to the extent necessary to resolve the controversy").

**The So What:**

**(1) Numerical claim terms are vulnerable to intrinsic-record narrowing.** After this case, patentees who state concentration values with a single significant figure but who emphasize "criticality" in the specification and prosecution history risk having their claims read at a much narrower precision than the scientific convention would provide. Downstream drafting lesson for pharma claim drafters: **be deliberate about significant figures + "about" qualifiers.** If you want the 0.0005%–0.0014% range, don't emphasize criticality at the fourth decimal place. If you want the 0.00095%–0.00104% range, write "0.0010%".

**(2) The Takeda "knew-how-to-express" canon extends further.** Combined with *PSN Illinois*'s canceled-claims doctrine, the majority creates a robust intrinsic-record narrowing regime for claim terms where inventors have shown (a) selectivity in use of approximation language and (b) deliberate abandonment of broader claims during prosecution. This will affect Hatch-Waxman claim-construction disputes in generic-drug cases.

**(3) Vacatur without deciding infringement.** The vacate-and-remand leaves Mylan to test its ANDA product against the new "0.00095% to 0.00104%" construction in district court. Under Taranto's overlap-exclusion analysis, Mylan's product would fall within even the narrower non-overlap range (0.00055% to 0.0014%) — Mylan's product tests within the 0.00095%–0.00104% band. So the practical effect of the vacatur may be nothing more than a delayed infringement finding.

**(4) Teach-away by implicit disparagement is affirmed as a viable route to nonobviousness.** Even absent explicit criticism of prior-art control formulations, expert testimony can establish that a skilled artisan would read the reference's own data as making the controls unsuitable — thereby "discouraging investigation into" those formulations. This is a plaintiff-friendly outcome for patentees defending against ANDA-based obviousness challenges built on prior-art comparative-test control groups.

## 4. Logical Flaws & Observations

- **Same judge, opposite reasoning register — Taranto in 21-1729 vs. 24-2242.** In [[24-2242|Erdemir v. ITC]] (2026), Taranto writes a unanimous, tight, function-over-form majority using the "true nature" doctrine to police pleading-through-relabeling — a **strong** function-based move. Here, in 21-1729 (2021), Taranto dissents to *insist on ordinary meaning* against the majority's function-based narrowing. **The judicial register is consistent** — careful boundary-drawing, resistance to under-defined doctrinal moves ("minor variations" here, mootness-confusion in Erdemir), disciplined analysis of whether the doctrinal tool actually reaches the case. But the *direction* of his function-vs-label move differs: in Erdemir he uses function against a re-labeled pleading; in AstraZeneca he defends label against a functionally-motivated rewrite. **This is a great data point for [[P13]] discussion — the principle of "map by function, not label" is not directional; it depends on what the label is actually doing and what the function actually is.**

- **The majority's "minor variations" is doctrinally under-specified.** Taranto's dissent nails this. "Minor variations" is a defined phrase only via a *second* construction move (equating it to "0.0010%" via an extra significant figure). Without that second move, the phrase does not clarify claim scope — it introduces uncertainty. This is a **resolution-without-choosing move disguised as a construction move**: the majority effectively says "0.001% is precise, with some fuzz" and then operationalizes the fuzz via a construction not directly stated in the opinion body. Compare [[25-1807]]'s explicit "we need not decide" declinations — 25-1807's majority owns the deferral. 21-1729's majority *performs* precision while depending on an implied secondary construction.

- **The Takeda canon is doing very aggressive work here.** *Takeda*'s "knew-how-to-express-but-chose-not-to" reasoning is essentially a **Sufficient Assumption** move: assume inventors' choices are deliberate, and their selective use of "about" implies deliberate narrow-scope choice for terms without "about." Taranto attacks this assumption directly — pointing out that AstraZeneca actively *withdrew* "about" language when narrowing to 0.001%, which he reads as choosing the precise significant-figure interval rather than as narrowing further to sub-significant-figure precision. **Both readings are plausible from the same prosecution-history facts.** This is a good LSAT-style exercise: **given the same evidence, argue both directions.**

- **The Vivid Techs "only what's necessary" principle is doing quiet work in Taranto's dissent.** He points out that even accepting Mylan's overlap argument, the maximum construction move is overlap-exclusion, and even that doesn't matter because Mylan's product falls within the narrower range regardless. So the majority's full rewrite exceeds what the controversy requires. **This is a doctrinal-restraint move worth naming** — the principle that courts should construe claims only to the extent necessary to resolve the actual dispute.

- **The teach-away holding is doctrinally lax.** The majority accepts implicit disparagement via expert testimony, even where Rogueda itself contains no explicit criticism. Under prior precedent (*Meiresonne*, *Galderma*), a reference must "criticize, discredit, or otherwise discourage" investigation. The majority stretches "discourage" to include "would a skilled artisan read the data and conclude these controls are unsuitable" — a subjective assessment mediated entirely through expert testimony. This gives defendants challenging obviousness a real advantage: cross-examine the expert on any prior-art data point that could be read as showing suboptimal performance. **Track this as a doctrinal drift** — pre-*KSR* teach-away doctrine was stricter about explicit disparagement.

- **Pre-*Loper Bright* environment shapes the analysis.** This is 2021, before *Loper Bright*'s "best reading" command. Compare to [[24-2242|Erdemir v. ITC]]'s explicit *Loper Bright* invocation. The claim-construction framework in *Phillips* has stayed stable across the *Chevron*-*Loper Bright* transition (claim construction has always been de novo for intrinsic-record cases per *Teva*), but the surrounding administrative-law environment has shifted meaningfully. Worth noting when reading pre-2024 cases: the interpretive canons look similar but the deference environment is different.

- **The 5% vs. 50% variation contrast is rhetorically effective but analytically loaded.** The majority observes that AstraZeneca's construction "would allow up to a 50% variation" (from 0.001% to 0.0014% is 40%; from 0.001% down to 0.0005% is 50%), while their construction allows only "a 5% variation." Framed that way, AstraZeneca's construction looks absurdly broad. But this reframing works only because the majority is expressing *variation as a percentage of the target value*, not as an *absolute concentration difference*. In absolute terms, both constructions are working at the fourth-fifth decimal place. **This is a "denominator choice" rhetorical move** — worth naming as a P1-family (topical vs. logical relevance) exercise. The relevant question is not "what % variation is claimed" but "at what precision does the underlying invention actually operate."

- **Panel-composition observation.** Taranto (heavyweight jurisdictional/administrative-law), Hughes (patent-doctrine specialist), Stoll (patent-doctrine specialist). Stoll + Hughes carrying the claim-construction majority makes sense — they're the patent-native voices. Taranto's dissent brings a discipline-of-scope perspective. **Two patent specialists outvote a heavyweight generalist on a claim-construction question**, which reads as intra-court signaling: patent-native judges own claim construction; Taranto's discipline is a valued outside voice but not controlling in this domain.

## 5. Follow-ups for Mr. C

- **LSAT reasoning angle (function vs. label at the claim-construction level — [[P13]] deployed both directions):** This case is a perfect two-sided exercise for the [[P13]] principle. **The majority's position:** function (stability data + criticality arguments) is what defines the invention; label ("0.001% written with one significant figure") is superficial and should give way to function. **The dissent's position:** the label IS the function — significant-figure convention IS what "0.001%" means to a skilled artisan; rewriting the label rewrites the invention. **Practice exercise:** in what other doctrinal contexts do we see function-vs-label disputes where both sides can plausibly invoke P13? Track when function should override and when it should not. Candidate rule: **function overrides label when the label was drafted before the function was fully understood; label overrides function when the label was drafted deliberately with knowledge of the function.** Apply that rule to this case: the specification and prosecution history show the inventors *knew* about the significant-figure convention and *knew* how to use "about" — so the label was deliberate. This suggests Taranto has the better of the argument.

- **LSAT reasoning angle (Sufficient Assumption structure of Takeda canon):** The *Takeda* "knew-how-to-express" canon is a canonical Sufficient Assumption structure. **Stimulus:** inventor used approximation language in some claims but not this one → conclusion: this term should be construed narrowly. **Missing premise:** selective use implies deliberate choice implies narrow-scope intent. Plug it in → conclusion follows. Taranto's dissent attacks the premise directly. **Practice exercise:** given the same "selective use of 'about'" fact, can you argue for a *broad* construction? (Yes: the inventors might have used "about" where they knew of specific variability concerns and dropped it where they trusted the ordinary meaning to convey the appropriate precision.)

- **LSAT reasoning angle (denominator choice as rhetorical move):** The majority's "5% vs. 50% variation" framing is a great [[P1]] topical-vs-logical exercise. What frame does the argument DEPEND on? What frame is being used? Do they match? Here: the argument depends on precision-of-the-invention (logical); the frame is percentage-of-target-value (topical). Naming this mismatch is a P1 win.

- **LSAT reasoning angle (Vivid Techs restraint principle):** "Construe only to the extent necessary to resolve the controversy" is a doctrinal restraint principle worth naming. This is a **procedural-economy** rule that appears in many doctrinal contexts (constitutional avoidance canon; standing doctrine's redressability prong; jurisdictional-avoidance rules). Track appearances across the corpus.

- **Doctrinal-evolution thread (teach-away):** *In re Mouttet* (2012) → *Galderma* (2013) → *Meiresonne* (2017) → this case (2021). The teach-away doctrine has drifted from requiring explicit disparagement toward permitting implicit disparagement via expert testimony reading unfavorable data. Track further evolution — post-2021 cases should show whether the "implicit via expert" route has been narrowed or expanded.

- **Cross-stitch with LRD-002 + LRD-003 corpus:**
  - **[[25-1807|Insulet v. EOFlow]]** — resolution-without-choosing was explicit ("we need not decide even under the more demanding Merck standard"). Here the resolution-without-choosing is *implicit* — the majority defines "minor variations" only via a secondary equivalence move. **Two flavors of the same H3 pattern:** explicit deferral vs. underspecified construction.
  - **[[24-2088|Ironburg v. Valve]]** — hindsight bias / skilled-searcher standard. Here the teach-away doctrine asks a similar question in reverse: what would a NON-hindsight-informed skilled artisan have concluded from Rogueda's data? The teach-away analysis is essentially anti-hindsight reasoning at the obviousness level, mirroring the anti-hindsight reasoning at the IPR-estoppel level in Ironburg.
  - **[[24-2369|Chafin v. OPM]]** — *Russello* canon (selective use implies deliberate choice). The *Takeda* canon here is *Russello* transplanted to claim-construction context. **Same reasoning shape, different doctrinal domain.** Both are "inference from selective use" moves.
  - **[[24-2242|Erdemir v. ITC]]** — same-judge (Taranto) contrast. In 24-2242 Taranto writes for a unanimous panel and uses function-over-form. Here he dissents to defend label. Judicial voice-consistency across nine years — worth naming.

- **Cross-stitch with [[24-1990|Jacobson v. US]] (LRD-003 companion):** Both cases are 3-judge Federal Circuit panels resolving statutory / linguistic construction disputes. Jacobson: interpret § 4208's "notwithstanding any other law, no court shall have jurisdiction" — clear textual signal, unanimous outcome. AstraZeneca: interpret "0.001%" — textually clean, but intrinsic-record contextualization creates a 2-1 split. **The lesson:** the number of interpretive canons available in a domain determines how contentious construction disputes become. Federal jurisdiction has few interpretive canons (presumption of review, clear-and-convincing rebuttal); patent claim construction has many (Phillips context, Takeda selective use, PSN Illinois canceled-claims, Fenner prosecution-history contribution). More canons = more room for disagreement = more 2-1 splits.

- **Candidate war-chest additions:**
  1. **"Rewrite as red flag" (Taranto restraint principle)** — when a construction requires adding significant figures, words, or precision the drafter did not use, the construction is rewriting the claim. Both *Vivid Techs* (construe only what's necessary) and *U.S. Surgical* (clarify, not modify) support this restraint.
  2. **"Overlap does not imply identity"** — two ranges that overlap can still be distinct in coverage. Denying-the-conjunction reasoning at the claim-construction level. Applies wherever two doctrinal categories share boundary regions.
  3. **"Denominator choice"** — percentage-based framings are rhetorically powerful but analytically loaded; always ask "percentage of what?" Applies to statistical arguments generally.
  4. **"Explicit vs. implicit disparagement in teach-away"** — a two-track doctrine where implicit disparagement is available via expert testimony. Track whether this drift continues or is narrowed.

- **Doctrinal thread on Taranto's voice:** Cases where Taranto has written or joined show a consistent register: careful scope-drawing, resistance to under-defined constructions, disciplined use of doctrinal tools. This is a **judicial voice pattern worth pinning** as a rubric for reading his opinions.

- **Watch thread:** Post-remand infringement analysis. The district court now applies the "0.00095% to 0.00104%" construction. Mylan's ANDA product concentration (per record) likely falls within this range, so remand may produce infringement on the new construction anyway. If so, this case illustrates a classic phenomenon: **narrower construction, same infringement result.** Track the docket for the remand outcome.

## Main Opinion Summary

Judge Stoll, joined by Judge Hughes, writes for the court on both issues. Judge Taranto joins the obviousness affirmance but dissents from the claim-construction holding. **Claim construction (vacatur):** The district court's construction of "0.001%" as encompassing 0.0005% to 0.0014% w/w PVP (per significant-figure convention) is vacated. The majority holds the intrinsic record supports a narrower construction — "that precise number, with only minor variations," i.e., 0.00095% to 0.00104% w/w. Three grounds: (1) the specification repeatedly emphasizes stability differentiation at the fourth decimal place, treating 0.001% as materially distinct from 0.0005% and other tested concentrations; (2) the prosecution history shows inventors narrowed multiple times to 0.001% while emphasizing "criticality" and dropping "about" qualifiers, per *Takeda* and *Fenner*; (3) *PSN Illinois* treats canceled claims (which included 0.0005%) as probative evidence that the canceled embodiment is not within the surviving claim scope. The stipulated judgment of infringement is vacated and remanded for infringement determination under the new construction. **Nonobviousness (affirmance):** The district court's factual finding that Rogueda's Controls 3 and 9 "taught away" from the claimed invention is not clearly erroneous. Expert testimony (Dr. Young) that a skilled artisan would view Rogueda's adhesion data ("drastic" reduction with novel formulations vs. controls) and particle-size data ("huge agglomerates") as showing the controls were "just unsuitable" for further experimentation supports **implicit disparagement**, even absent explicit criticism in Rogueda itself. AFFIRMED-IN-PART, VACATED-IN-PART, AND REMANDED. No costs.

## Dissenting Opinion Summary

Judge Taranto dissents from Part I of the majority (claim construction) and would affirm the district court's significant-figure construction. Four grounds: **(1) Ordinary meaning is the significant-figure interval.** Standard scientific convention (*Viskase*, *Valeant*, *U.S. Philips*) gives "0.001%" the well-defined interval 0.0005% to 0.0014% based on rounding rules and single-significant-figure precision. The district court so held; the majority does not directly dispute this. **(2) "Minor variations" fails as a construction.** Without further precision, "minor variations" adds uncertainty rather than clarifying claim scope — working against the core purpose of claim construction (*U.S. Surgical*, *Arlington Industries*, *O2 Micro*, *Avid Technology*). It effectively reinstates the "about" language AstraZeneca actively removed during prosecution. **(3) Equating "minor variations" to "0.0010%" rewrites the claim.** The specification and prosecution history uniformly used one significant figure when describing PVP concentrations — never two. This case is distinguishable from *Viskase*, where the patentees themselves used an extra significant figure in prosecution history. **(4) The 0.001%/0.0005% overlap does not negate the distinction.** Two ranges can overlap while remaining distinct; the specification's differentiated test results are compatible with both terms retaining their significant-figure meanings. Even if overlap justified narrowing, the maximum move would be overlap-exclusion (0.00055% to 0.0014%), not full rewrite to 0.0010%. And Mylan's ANDA product falls within even the narrower non-overlap range, so *Vivid Techs.'*s "construe only to the extent necessary" principle counsels not reaching the fuller rewrite. Taranto would affirm the judgment of infringement as well as the judgment of no invalidity.
