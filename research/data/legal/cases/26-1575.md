# Megan Jackler, Brandon Jaroch v. Department of Justice, Director of OPM, No. 26-1575 (Fed. Cir. June 17, 2026) — Per Curiam Order

## 1. The Issue

Whether the petition for initial hearing en banc filed by petitioners Megan Jackler and Brandon Jaroch, seeking review of three Merit Systems Protection Board decisions (Nos. CF-0752-26-0069-I-1, DA-0752-25-0328-I-1, DA-0752-25-0330-I-1) concerning federal employee personnel actions, warrants en banc consideration by the full Federal Circuit.

(Note: this is a *procedural* issue regarding hearing en banc, not a substantive legal issue on the merits. The underlying merits of the MSPB challenges will be briefed in the en banc proceeding to follow per the schedule set out in the Order.)

## 2. Rule Mapping

**Federal Circuit en banc procedure:** Fed. Cir. R. 35; Fed. R. App. P. 35 — en banc hearing requires a poll of circuit judges in regular active service; majority vote of the active judges in regular active service is required to grant. Hearing en banc is appropriate when (1) consideration is necessary to secure or maintain uniformity of court decisions, or (2) the proceeding involves a question of exceptional importance.

(The Order itself recites that "the court decided that the petition for review warrants en banc consideration" but does not articulate WHICH ground supports the grant — uniformity or exceptional importance.)

## 3. Synthesis: The "Why" and "So What"

**The Why:** *Not extractable from this document type.* The Order grants en banc consideration without providing rationale. The Federal Circuit's practice when granting en banc review is to dispose by short order without explaining the merits-side reasoning that motivated the grant — that reasoning will emerge (if at all) in the eventual en banc opinion. What we can say: the court accepted amicus briefs from a substantial coalition (American Federation of Government Employees; six federal-employee/labor unions; Justice Connection; six U.S. Senators across both parties; Whistleblower Aid; MSPB Professional Association), which signals broad stakeholder interest in the underlying MSPB-procedure issues. The Government invited a response to the en banc petition, also indicating the court took the issues seriously enough to want adversarial briefing before the poll.

**The So What:** The en banc grant itself is the news. Initial hearing en banc (as opposed to en banc rehearing after panel decision) is relatively rare at the Federal Circuit. The schedule (en banc opening brief due July 14, 2026; response 45 days later; reply 30 days after that) means an en banc opinion is plausible by late 2026 or early 2027. The participating-judges list (11 of 12 active judges; Judge Newman did not participate, consistent with her ongoing administrative suspension status) is notable. The MSPB doctrine area is a relatively quiet corner of the Federal Circuit's docket compared to patents; an en banc treatment will reshape it.

## 4. Logical Flaws & Observations

- **Per curiam orders do not give us a synthesis to extract.** Unlike a substantive opinion, this Order provides only the disposition (granted), the schedule (briefing dates), and the procedural framework (amicus briefing without leave required). The 5-section Field Clerk template assumes a substantive opinion with rationale; ORDERS and many other docket entries (procedural orders, judgments, costs orders) won't fit cleanly.
- **The amicus-curiae roster is itself informative.** The breadth of participating amici — federal-employee unions across multiple jurisdictions, six Senators including both Democratic and Republican leaning (Van Hollen, Alsobrooks, Kaine, Kim, Peters, Warner — all Democrats here, actually), Whistleblower Aid, MSPB Professional Association — suggests the underlying merits raise federal-employee due-process or whistleblower-protection issues likely connected to broader Trump-administration personnel policies. The MSPB docket numbers (CF-0752, DA-0752) involve adverse personnel actions (5 U.S.C. ch. 75 jurisdiction); the substantive issue is likely to involve scope of MSPB review or due-process protections.
- **Judge Newman's non-participation is noted in footnote 1** without explanation. This is consistent with the ongoing administrative suspension of Judge Newman that has been a notable Federal Circuit governance issue. Worth tracking that even high-stakes en banc grants now proceed routinely without her.

## Main Opinion Summary

Per curiam order of the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, signed by Clerk Jarrett B. Perlow on June 17, 2026. The court grants the petition for initial hearing en banc filed by Megan Jackler and Brandon Jaroch (collectively, petitioners) seeking review of three Merit Systems Protection Board decisions. The court invited and received a response from the Government, and accepted amicus briefs from a broad coalition. The petition and response were referred to the circuit judges in regular active service; a poll was requested and taken; the court determined the petition warrants en banc consideration. The Order grants the petition, directs new briefing under specified deadlines (opening brief due July 14, 2026; response within 45 days; reply within 30 days of response; joint appendix within 7 days of reply), and orders that amicus briefs may be filed without consent or leave of the court, with timing keyed to opening and response briefs. Oral argument is to be scheduled later. Judge Newman did not participate.
