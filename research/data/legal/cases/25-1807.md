# Insulet Corp. v. EOFlow, Co. Ltd., No. 25-1807 (Fed. Cir. May 28, 2026)

## 1. The Issue

Three interlocking issues across jurisdiction, statute of limitations, and pleading standards: (1) Whether the Federal Circuit has jurisdiction over an appeal in which the underlying patent claims were dismissed "without prejudice" after a jury verdict on a non-patent trade secret claim — specifically whether such a dismissal functions as one *with* prejudice because the six-year patent SOL had run; (2) what standard governs accrual of the DTSA statute of limitations (18 U.S.C. § 1836(d)) — the inquiry-notice standard or the *Merck* discovery rule — and whether under either standard Insulet's DTSA trade-secret-misappropriation claim was time-barred when filed in August 2023; (3) whether, under DTSA's "continuing misappropriation constitutes a single claim" provision, the SOL must be analyzed trade-secret-by-trade-secret or whether discovery of misappropriation as to one trade secret starts the clock for all related secrets shared in the same disclosure relationship.

## 2. Rule Mapping

**Controlling statute (DTSA SOL):** 18 U.S.C. § 1836(d) — "A civil action under [the DTSA] may not be commenced later than 3 years after the date on which the misappropriation with respect to which the action would relate is discovered or by the exercise of reasonable diligence should have been discovered. For purposes of this subsection, a continuing misappropriation constitutes a single claim of misappropriation."

**Jurisdiction:** 28 U.S.C. § 1295(a)(1) — Federal Circuit has exclusive jurisdiction over civil actions arising under acts of Congress relating to patents. A dismissal "without prejudice" of patent claims may function as a dismissal *with* prejudice — and therefore preserve Federal Circuit jurisdiction — when the patent SOL (35 U.S.C. § 286, six years) has run. The test is *functional, not semantic* (*Chamberlain Group v. Skylink Techs.*, 381 F.3d 1178 (Fed. Cir. 2004)).

**Pleading/proof standard for trade secret misappropriation (the "access-plus-similarity" framework adopted by the panel):** A plaintiff possesses sufficient facts to state a DTSA claim when it has knowledge that (1) the alleged misappropriator has access to the alleged trade secrets — typically through a former employee of the trade secret owner — AND (2) there is similarity between the trade secrets and the alleged misappropriator's competing product. Circumstantial evidence of access plus similarity is sufficient (*Harbor Bus. Compliance Corp. v. Firstbase.io*, 152 F.4th 516 (3d Cir. 2025); *Oakwood Labs. v. Thanoo*, 999 F.3d 892 (3d Cir. 2021); *Leggett & Platt v. Hickory Springs Mfg.*, 285 F.3d 1353 (Fed. Cir. 2002)).

**Single-claim accrual rule (continuing misappropriation):** Per § 1836(d), the first discovered (or discoverable) misappropriation of a trade secret commences the limitations period for all related trade secrets disclosed by the same person during substantially the same time period in connection with the same relationship (*Intermedics v. Ventritex*, 822 F. Supp. 634 (N.D. Cal. 1993); *MGA Ent. v. Mattel*, 254 Cal. Rptr. 3d 314 (Cal. Ct. App. 2019); *Gognat v. Ellsworth*, 259 P.3d 497 (Colo. 2011)). The DTSA explicitly "rejects a continuing wrong approach" inherited from the 1985 UTSA.

**Choice-of-standard for discovery (inquiry-notice vs Merck):** The court declines to resolve, finding the SOL expired even under the more demanding *Merck v. Reynolds*, 559 U.S. 633 (2010), discovery standard. Under Merck, the clock starts when plaintiff did discover (or with reasonable diligence should have discovered) facts underlying the cause of action — not merely when inquiry would have begun.

## 3. Synthesis: The "Why" and "So What"

**The Why:** Three holdings drive the reversal. **First**, the panel preserves Federal Circuit jurisdiction by treating Insulet's "without prejudice" dismissal of its patent claims as functionally *with* prejudice — because the six-year § 286 SOL had run with respect to the alleged June 2018 acts of importation by the time of the January 2025 dismissal, refiling on that same act was barred. The court explicitly extends this functional rule to *voluntary* dismissals (citing *Hilton Int'l v. Union de Trabajadores*, 833 F.2d 10 (1st Cir. 1987)). **Second**, the panel applies the access-plus-similarity framework and finds Insulet had sufficient knowledge by 2018-2019 to plead its DTSA claim: (a) Insulet knew former employee Steve DiIanni — a "veritable repository" of Omnipod design knowledge — was working for EOFlow on the EOPatch 2 by March 2019; (b) Insulet's own employees at the June 2018 ADA conference observed and internally documented that EOPatch 2 "bore a stunning resemblance" to the Omnipod ("EOFlow has cloned our product"; "looks almost identical to Omnipod"). Combined with the EOFlow Korean IPO prospectus (publicly available July 2020), these put Insulet on actual knowledge of access-plus-similarity well before the August 3, 2020 critical date. **Third**, the panel applies single-claim accrual under § 1836(d) — because DiIanni allegedly disclosed all four asserted trade secrets (CAD files, soft cannula, design history file, occlusion-detection algorithm) to EOFlow during the same March-May 2018 consultancy for the same purpose (designing EOPatch 2), the SOL clock that started for the CAD files also started for the other secrets, including the occlusion-detection algorithm trade secret that was used only internally and would not have outwardly manifested in EOFlow's product.

**The So What:** This opinion is doctrinally important on three fronts. **(1) Voluntary-dismissal-with-prejudice functional rule:** Patent plaintiffs who voluntarily dismiss patent claims after winning on related non-patent claims can no longer assume the Federal Circuit lacks appellate jurisdiction — the question is whether *refiling* is barred by SOL, not how the dismissal is labeled. This will affect appellate strategy in mixed patent/non-patent litigation. **(2) Access-plus-similarity at the pleading stage:** The panel formalizes a relatively plaintiff-friendly pleading standard for DTSA misappropriation, but in doing so creates a corresponding accelerator on SOL — once a plaintiff has access-plus-similarity knowledge, the clock starts even before they've gathered the specific evidence needed to *prove* misappropriation. The panel is clear: "Knowledge of such details as the nominal dimensions of EOPatch 2 is unnecessary to plead a trade secret misappropriation claim." **(3) Single-accrual sweep:** Trade-secret owners who suspect misappropriation of *any* secret disclosed in a single confidential-relationship rupture must sue promptly for *all* related secrets — they cannot "compartmentalize" suspicion (*MGA*, 254 Cal. Rptr. 3d at 321) and reserve later-discovered trade secrets for later suits. This is a significant litigation-management constraint. Combined effect: trade-secret plaintiffs face a tighter SOL squeeze with broader sweep, which favors defendants in long-tail misappropriation disputes.

## 4. Logical Flaws & Observations

- **The choice-of-standard duck.** The panel explicitly declines to resolve whether DTSA uses the *Merck* discovery rule or the older inquiry-notice standard, ruling that "even under the more demanding *Merck* standard" Insulet loses. This is a familiar judicial move — resolve the case, preserve flexibility — but **Prost's dissent has a real point here:** the panel's actual analysis (focused on when Insulet *should have begun investigating*) looks more like inquiry-notice than discovery rule. The majority's "access-plus-similarity is sufficient knowledge to plead" framing arguably collapses the distinction between the two standards, even while claiming to apply the more demanding one. This is a doctrinal sleight worth tracking — future DTSA cases will cite this as *Merck*-rule precedent, even though the panel never committed to *Merck*.

- **The dissent's strongest move.** Prost (a heavyweight in this corner of doctrine) argues the majority "flips the JMOL standard on its head and accepts EOFlow's view of the facts over Insulet's." On JMOL review post-jury-verdict, the panel is required to view evidence in the light most favorable to Insulet, draw all factual inferences in its favor, and not weigh credibility. The majority does conduct a detailed evidence analysis — but it characterizes Insulet's contrary evidence (cannulas "cut off" of ADA samples; Korean-language 500-page prospectus filed weeks before the critical date; Insulet executives' subjective testimony about not knowing of misappropriation until later) as "conclusory" or "subjective." Prost replies that ALL witness testimony is in some sense subjective, and credibility weighing is for the jury. There's a real procedural-posture tension here that the majority does not fully resolve.

- **The dissent on access-plus-similarity itself.** Prost argues the framework as the majority articulates it produces "absurd results" — "Employees frequently move between companies working on similar products. Under the majority's framework, a plaintiff's statute-of-limitations clock starts prematurely whenever a former employee joins a competitor that makes a superficially similar product." This is a serious policy concern. The majority's response is essentially that *suspicion* is enough to plead; the plaintiff doesn't need *proof*. But the line between "suspicion" and "fact-finding-eligible knowledge" remains fuzzy in operation.

- **Single-accrual sweep applied to the occlusion-detection algorithm is the most aggressive move.** The occlusion-detection algorithm was used only internally to develop EOPatch 2 — it would not have outwardly manifested in the competing product the way the CAD-file-derived physical features did. The majority concedes that "absent direct proof of a misappropriator's access to the trade secret, the statute of limitations would never expire for a trade secret that could not be discovered based on publicly available information" if each secret were analyzed separately. Their solution: yoke the occlusion-detection algorithm's SOL to the CAD-file SOL because both were disclosed in the same DiIanni consultancy. This is functionally sensible (avoiding the "never expires" problem) but doctrinally creative — § 1836(d)'s "single claim" provision becomes a sweep mechanism, not just a continuing-wrong-rejection device.

- **Newman's continuing absence.** Not mentioned in this opinion (no recusal noted in the panel composition: Dyk, Prost, Reyna). But worth noting that this Federal Circuit panel has Judges Dyk and Prost in a meaningful 2-1 split on a high-stakes commercial case — exactly the kind of case where senior judge participation matters. Track the dissent: Prost is Chief Judge emerita; her dissent will carry weight in subsequent DTSA appeals.

## 5. Follow-ups for Mr. C

- **LSAT reasoning angle (statutory construction):** This opinion is rich in classic legal-reasoning structures worth dissecting for SR sessions. The majority's resolution-without-choosing technique ("we need not decide ... even under the more demanding standard") is a common appellate move; recognizing when a court is deferring vs. resolving is a key analytical skill. Practice exercise: identify three other places in this opinion where the panel chooses *not* to resolve a question (e.g., footnote 9 declining to reach whether design history file constitutes a trade secret; declining to resolve the inquiry-notice vs Merck choice; declining to resolve other JMOL grounds raised by EOFlow). Why does each get deferred?

- **LSAT reasoning angle (canon application):** The single-claim accrual analysis is a textbook application of statutory canons — the panel uses the DTSA's "in light of" relationship to the UTSA (*George v. McDonough*: "Where Congress employs a term of art obviously transplanted from another legal source, it brings the old soil with it") to import UTSA case law. Worth knowing as a transplanted-term canon for LSAT reading comp.

- **Doctrinal-evolution thread:** *Merck v. Reynolds* (2010, Supreme Court) → *Oakwood Labs v. Thanoo* (3d Cir. 2021) → *Ingenico v. IOENGINE* (Fed. Cir. 2025) → this case. The DTSA SOL jurisprudence is still being built. Track whether circuits other than the Federal Circuit converge on the access-plus-similarity framework or take a stricter knowledge requirement.

- **Cross-stitch with prior corpus:** This case shares a panel with [[24-2088|Ironburg v. Valve]] in one respect — Judge Stark sits on Ironburg's panel; Prost sits here. Different judges, different posture (patent IPR estoppel vs DTSA trade secret), but both opinions push on the question of *what discovery-of-facts is sufficient to start a clock*. Worth pairing them in an SR session on "discovery-rule mechanics across federal statutes."

- **Watch thread:** EOFlow's appeal in the First Circuit is now moot per footnote 2; the case ends here unless certiorari follows. The $25.8M + $33.6M judgment is vacated. Insulet's downstream options: refile any patent claims for acts of infringement *not* time-barred by § 286 (per the majority's footnote on the "series of discrete infringing acts" rule from *Arellano* and *Petrella*) — possible but narrowed.

## Main Opinion Summary

Judge Dyk, joined by Judge Reyna, reverses the district court's denial of EOFlow's JMOL motion on the DTSA claim. **Jurisdiction:** The "without prejudice" dismissal of Insulet's patent claims functioned as a dismissal *with* prejudice because the six-year § 286 SOL had run on the alleged June 2018 acts of importation — preserving Federal Circuit jurisdiction. **Statute of limitations:** Under either the inquiry-notice or *Merck* discovery standard (court declines to choose), Insulet's DTSA claim is time-barred. Applying the access-plus-similarity framework, Insulet had sufficient knowledge by March 2019 — DiIanni working for EOFlow on EOPatch 2 + observed striking similarities at the 2018 ADA conference + EOFlow's Korean IPO prospectus disclosing key design features by July 2020 — to plead trade secret misappropriation. The critical date was August 3, 2020 (three years before filing). **Single-claim accrual:** Under § 1836(d)'s "continuing misappropriation constitutes a single claim" provision, the SOL clock for related trade secrets disclosed in the same confidential-relationship rupture (here, DiIanni's March-May 2018 consultancy disclosing all four asserted secrets to EOFlow for designing EOPatch 2) starts when the *first* misappropriation is discovered or discoverable. The occlusion-detection algorithm — used only internally and not outwardly manifest in EOPatch 2 — is therefore time-barred along with the CAD-file, soft-cannula, and design-history-file trade secrets. REVERSED. Costs to EOFlow.

## Dissenting Opinion Summary

Judge Prost dissents, joining only Part I (jurisdiction) of the majority opinion. Four major critiques: **(1) Standard conflation:** The DTSA's text plainly adopts the discovery rule, not inquiry-notice; the majority's actual analysis focuses on when Insulet *should have begun investigating* (inquiry-notice), not when it *did or should have discovered* the misappropriation (discovery rule). **(2) Access-plus-similarity overreach:** The majority's framework — that a plaintiff has sufficient facts to plead misappropriation merely from (i) a former employee with trade-secret knowledge working at a competitor and (ii) a similar-looking competing product — is not supported by the cases cited (*Leggett & Platt* concerned summary judgment, not pleading; *Sokol* explicitly held similarity-plus-access was still just "suspicion," not knowledge of misuse). Under the majority's framework, plaintiffs face a premature rush to the courthouse based on mere suspicion. **(3) JMOL standard inversion:** On post-verdict JMOL review, the panel is required to view evidence in the light most favorable to the prevailing party (Insulet) and not weigh credibility. The majority instead conducts its own evidentiary weighing — characterizing Insulet's contrary evidence (cannulas "cut off" of ADA conference samples; Korean-language 500-page prospectus available mere weeks before critical date; Insulet employee testimony about not knowing of misappropriation until later) as "conclusory" or "subjective." This is fact-finding by an appellate court. **(4) Occlusion-detection algorithm specifically:** The single-accrual sweep is reaching for a theory EOFlow did not press on appeal. The occlusion-detection algorithm was not in Insulet's original complaint; Insulet discovered the misappropriation in discovery; EOFlow witnesses themselves admitted there was nothing in the Korean prospectus that would have alerted Insulet to occlusion-detection-algorithm misappropriation. The dissent would have affirmed the jury verdict on this trade secret. Prost would also have reached EOFlow's other arguments (design-history-file trade-secret specificity, reasonable measures jury instruction, damages award) and "would have agreed with EOFlow that Insulet's design-history-file trade secret was severely overbroad" — interesting signal that Prost shares some skepticism of Insulet's claim shape even while disagreeing on the SOL analysis.
