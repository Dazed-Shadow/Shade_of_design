# Carolyn W. Hafeman v. Google LLC, Microsoft Corporation, Nos. 24-1600 through 24-1605 (Fed. Cir. June 5, 2026)

## 1. The Issue

Three issues: (1) Whether the Board's failure to address the patent owner's Sotera-stipulation-violation arguments in the Final Written Decisions (FWDs) constitutes appealable error, or whether such challenges are barred by 35 U.S.C. § 314(d)'s prohibition on judicial review of institution-related determinations; (2) whether the Board correctly construed (or implicitly construed) the "without assistance by a user" limitation in the challenged patents to permit a user to establish the underlying internet connection; (3) whether the Board's rejection of secondary considerations of non-obviousness (commercial success, praise, copying) was supported by substantial evidence.

## 2. Rule Mapping

**Controlling statute on review-bar:** 35 U.S.C. § 314(d) — "The determination by the Director whether to institute an inter partes review under this section shall be final and nonappealable."

**Governing precedent on § 314(d) scope:**
- *Cuozzo Speed Techs., LLC v. Lee*, 579 U.S. 261, 275 (2016) — § 314(d) bars review of matters "closely tied to the application and interpretation of statutes related to" the institution decision.
- *Thryv, Inc. v. Click-To-Call Techs., LP*, 590 U.S. 45, 52 (2020) — § 314(d) bars review "even labeled as an appeal from the final written decision" where the "contention remained, essentially, that the agency should have refused to institute."
- *Federal Express Corp. v. Qualcomm Inc.*, 174 F.4th 910 (Fed. Cir. 2026); *Ethanol Boosting Sys., LLC v. Ford Motor Co.*, 162 F.4th 1151 (Fed. Cir. 2025); *IGT v. Zynga Inc.*, 144 F.4th 1357 (Fed. Cir. 2025) — challenges with "institution as [their] direct, immediate, express subject" remain barred regardless of how framed.

**Claim construction:** Plain language of claim controls; specification informs but cannot contradict claim text (*Prima Tek II*, 318 F.3d 1143; *Baxalta v. Genentech*, 972 F.3d 1341).

**Secondary considerations nexus requirement:** A presumption of nexus arises only when the secondary-considerations evidence is "tied to a specific product that embodies the claimed features and is coextensive with them" (*Volvo Penta*, 81 F.4th 1202); absent the presumption, the patentee must show the evidence is the "direct result of the unique characteristics of the claimed invention."

## 3. Synthesis: The "Why" and "So What"

**The Why:** The court holds Hafeman's Sotera-violation challenge is barred even though the LG district-court violation occurred *after* institution, because the remedy she sought (vacate FWDs and dismiss IPRs) traces directly back to a consideration that drove institution itself. The Federal Express and Ethanol Boosting line of cases is dispositive: a litigant cannot escape § 314(d) by "pointing to a different agency action" if the relief sought necessarily collapses into a challenge to institution propriety. On claim construction, the court rejects Hafeman's reading of "without assistance" as requiring the user not establish even an internet connection — the limitation modifies "initiating or changing return information," not the antecedent step of establishing the communication channel itself. On secondary considerations, the court applies substantial-evidence deference to the Board's findings that praise and commercial-success evidence lacked nexus to the claimed features.

**The So What:** This is a significant procedural-bar opinion. It closes off a creative theory of post-institution appellate review (using post-institution stipulation violations as a hook) and reinforces that § 314(d) is enforced by examining the substance of relief sought, not the label affixed to the claim. For patent owners facing IPR challenges where the Board institutes based on a Sotera-stipulation representation, post-institution stipulation breach is now firmly outside Federal Circuit review — the remedy must come from the district court itself, or the Board must be persuaded to revisit institution voluntarily. The claim-construction holding is a clean illustration of the modifier-scope principle, useful as a model for how courts read "without X" limitations narrowly to the action they textually modify.

## 4. Logical Flaws & Observations

- **The line between "challenge to institution" and "challenge to post-institution conduct" is more contestable than the opinion acknowledges.** Hafeman's strongest argument is that LG's violation of the Sotera stipulation occurred *after* institution and was independently litigated and found in district court. The Federal Circuit's distinguishing of *Uniloc* (no-estoppel rulings) and *Facebook v. Windy City* (joinder rulings) on the basis that those were "post-institution" and "separate" from institution is itself a category that turns on the Federal Circuit's judgment about what counts as "separate." The court's effective test — "does the relief sought collapse back into institution?" — is functional and may be over-inclusive, since most post-institution remedies will eventually trace back to institution-related considerations.
- **The intervention by the USPTO Director is notable** and signals the agency's institutional interest in protecting § 314(d) from erosion. Acting Director Squires intervened in support of the petitioners' position that the challenge is barred. This is procedurally interesting: agency intervention in adverse-party patent appeals to reinforce jurisdictional bars is a growing pattern worth tracking.
- **The "borderline frivolous" characterization** of Hafeman's secondary-considerations argument (Part C, page 17) is unusually sharp judicial language for what amounts to a one-sentence appellate argument unsupported by record citation. The opinion uses this to underscore that appellate arguments require record support, not just assertion — a useful precedent on appellate-argument-waiver.

## 5. Follow-ups for Mr. C

- **LSAT reasoning angle:** Statutory-bar analysis is a recurring LSAT logical-reasoning structure. The pattern is: (i) general rule + bar, (ii) attempted exception/end-run, (iii) test for whether the end-run is genuine or pretextual. Practice exercise: identify what kind of argument the court makes when it says "she purports to challenge the FWDs, but the heart of her challenge goes to institution." This is a *substance-over-form* argument, formally analogous to piercing-the-corporate-veil or look-through doctrines in tax/contracts.
- **Watch thread:** The Federal Express, Ethanol Boosting, and IGT v. Zynga trio plus this opinion form a coherent doctrinal block. The next appeal that tests § 314(d) at its margins — particularly involving genuinely post-institution Board conduct (e.g., spoliation, new sanction issues) — will be where the bar's limits are tested. Atlanta Gas Light (cited in passing) is the cited exception for pure sanctions decisions.
- **Cross-stitch with 24-2088 Ironburg:** Both opinions involve IPR-related doctrine but at different procedural moments. Ironburg is about post-IPR civil litigation (§ 315(e)(2) estoppel); Hafeman is about IPR appellate review itself (§ 314(d)). Together they show the Federal Circuit policing IPR-related litigation strictly along statutory boundaries.

## Main Opinion Summary

Judge Hughes, for a panel of Dyk, Hughes, and Stoll, dismisses Hafeman's Sotera-related appeal for lack of jurisdiction under § 314(d) and affirms the Board's claim construction and secondary-considerations findings on the merits. As to the Sotera challenge: Hafeman's relief (vacate the FWDs and remand for dismissal) seeks to terminate the IPRs for reasons tracing back to the Sotera stipulation that drove institution — making her challenge institutional in substance even though framed as a post-institution APA challenge to the FWDs. The Federal Express line of cases controls. As to claim construction of "without assistance by a user": the limitation modifies the action of "initiating or changing return information," not the antecedent step of establishing internet connectivity; Hafeman's broader reading conflicts with claim text, specification (which contemplates user-initiated internet connection via the McAfee-style auto-update analogy), and prosecution history (which targeted only the Broyles-style interrupt-key step). As to secondary considerations: substantial evidence supports the Board's findings that Hafeman failed to establish nexus (praise targeted the unpatented verbal-alarm feature; commercial-success evidence was not tied to claimed merits; copying allegations were not raised below). DISMISSED-IN-PART, AFFIRMED-IN-PART. Costs against appellant.
