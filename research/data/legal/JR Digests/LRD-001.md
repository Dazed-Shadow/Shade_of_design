Facts: Ollnova technologies filed four infridgement claims against ECOBEE related to wireless communications with smart thermostats in buildings.

No direct infridgements were referenced when the jury awarded the plaintiff 11.5M USD in damages. 'The jury only had to decide on the "inventive concept" without instruction of the "abstract idea". 

Issue: When the courts asked the jury to assess the patent infridgement validity on the generalized concept of the invention, does that violate the 7th amendment requirement for a unanimous verdict? | Section 101 Jury Instructions


Ruling: The federal court vacated the judgment awarded by the trail court. They held the verdict was 'defective' as it prevented the jury to be unanimous as to which patent was infridged. (Basket to item) The jury instructions were found to be legally erroneous. 

- Analysis:  JR Copied the below as I would like this further explained for the many to one. (First: Summary of individual voting on each distinct item | Optis Cellular v. Apple)

**The "Any Patent" Problem:** The Federal Circuit reaffirmed its reasoning from _Optis Cellular v. Apple_, stating that a general verdict allows for a "composite ruling" where jurors might disagree on which patent was infringed (e.g., Juror A thinks Patent 1 was infringed; Juror B thinks Patent 2 was infringed) but still vote "Yes" on the single question. This fails to ensure that ecobee was unanimously found liable for any single specific act of infringement. [[1](https://www.fenwick.com/insights/publications/ollnova-v-ecobee-federal-circuit-reaffirms-requirements-patent-jury-instructions-and-verdict-forms), [2](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/ip-law/ollnovas-11-5-million-patent-win-wiped-out-over-bad-jury-form)]

**Blind Eligibility Analysis:** On the § 101 issue, the court explained that the "inventive concept" inquiry requires comparing the claim elements _not_ directed to the abstract idea against the state of the art. By failing to define the abstract idea for the jury, the District Court essentially asked the jury to perform a legal analysis without the necessary legal parameters, rendering the verdict on validity defective.
  

- Considered Authorities: (Conclusion replacement)

**Alice § 101 framework (Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank Int'l, 573 U.S. 208 (2014)):**
- **Step one:** Are the claims directed to a patent-ineligible concept (abstract idea, law of nature, natural phenomenon)?
- **Step two:** Do the additional elements (individually and as ordered combination) transform the claim into a patent-eligible application?
- **Key limitation:** "The abstract idea itself cannot supply the inventive concept" (*Trading Techs. Int'l, Inc. v. IBG LLC*, 921 F.3d 1378 (Fed. Cir. 2019); *BSG Tech LLC v. Buyseasons, Inc.*, 899 F.3d 1281 (Fed. Cir. 2018)).

### Recommendation for how to test what I learned
MR C (Opus@COS) recommended angles:

#### [[24-1600]]
**LSAT reasoning angle:** Statutory-bar analysis is a recurring LSAT logical-reasoning structure. The pattern is: (i) general rule + bar, (ii) attempted exception/end-run, (iii) test for whether the end-run is genuine or pretextual. Practice exercise: identify what kind of argument the court makes when it says "she purports to challenge the FWDs, but the heart of her challenge goes to institution." This is a *substance-over-form* argument, formally analogous to piercing-the-corporate-veil or look-through doctrines in tax/contracts.
- **Watch thread:** The Federal Express, Ethanol Boosting, and IGT v. Zynga trio plus this opinion form a coherent doctrinal block. The next appeal that tests § 314(d) at its margins — particularly involving genuinely post-institution Board conduct (e.g., spoliation, new sanction issues) — will be where the bar's limits are tested. Atlanta Gas Light (cited in passing) is the cited exception for pure sanctions decisions.


#### [[25-1045]]
 **LSAT reasoning angle:** This opinion is a multi-issue dispositional opinion — a less common LSAT logical-reasoning structure but a critical real-world skill. Practice exercise: outline the four issues, identify how the court's vacate-and-remand on issues 1-2 affects whether it needs to reach issues 5-6. The "we do not reach" pattern is a constructive use of conditional reasoning that LSAT logical-reasoning rarely tests but bar-exam essay writing requires constantly.
- **Doctrinal-evolution thread:** Alice § 101 has been a doctrinal mess since 2014. The combination of (i) ChargePoint's "characterizations of the abstract idea" approach, (ii) Aatrix/Cooperative Entertainment's permission to deny motions on step two without ruling step one, and (iii) this opinion's caution about doing exactly that pre-trial — these create a complex strategic landscape for both patent owners and challengers. Worth tracking how district courts implement footnote 7's guidance.
- **The '887 patent eligibility holding is particularly worth knowing in detail** — the court's articulation of why polling-interval + conditional-transmission constraints are "specific technique" rather than "abstract result" is a model for how to frame technological-improvement claims to clear Alice step one.

#### [[24-2316]]
**LSAT reasoning angle:** Standing doctrine is heavy in conditional reasoning ("if A and B and C, then standing exists; the plaintiff must establish each of A, B, and C"). This case tests injury-in-fact specifically. Practice exercise: trace the court's reasoning for *why* IRAdvocates' alleged injury fails — it's a "type of injury" argument (their injury is the wrong type) rather than a "magnitude of injury" argument (their injury isn't big enough). What is the formal logical difference?
- **Doctrinal-evolution thread:** Hippocratic Medicine is recent (2024) and lower courts are still working out its reach. The Federal Circuit's narrow reading of Havens here is one data point; circuit-split potential exists, especially in the D.C. Circuit (which decided Abigail Alliance pre-Hippocratic-Medicine). Worth tracking for next 12-24 months.
- **Cross-domain connection:** This is the only non-patent case in your current corpus. The standing analysis applies across all federal litigation; the substantive Tariff Act / forced-labor framework is a discrete domain. For LSAT practice, the standing analysis is the transferable skill; for legal-domain breadth, the human-rights/trade angle is the novel content. Worth flagging that the Federal Circuit's docket is much broader than just patents.
