## Case: 21-1729 — AstraZeneca v. Mylan

*Condensed Treatment*
### Rough Notes (JR)



#### Issue

Does the claim term "0.001%" encompass a standard significant-figure range (0.0005% to 0.0014%), or is it limited to precisely 0.001% with only minor variations?

#### Facts

AstraZeneca sued Mylan for infringing patents on its Symbicort inhaler. The core dispute centers on the required concentration of the excipient PVP K25, which the patent claims at "0.001% w/w". 

The original patent application used the phrase "about 0.0005 to about 0.05 %w/w," which the inventors later narrowed to specifically claim 0.001% to overcome prior art rejections by the patent examiner


#### Reasoning

Ruling:
The ordinary meaning of a claim term is determined in the context of the entire patent, including the written description and the prosecution history, rather than reading the number in an abstract vacuum

Analysis:
While standard scientific rounding would normally give "0.001%" a wider variance, the court noted the inventors specifically distinguished 0.001% from 0.0005% in their stability testing. 
Furthermore, during prosecution, the inventors intentionally deleted the word "about" and repeatedly argued to the examiner that exactly 0.001% was "critical" for stability to overcome obviousness rejections.

Because the inventors demonstrated they knew how to use words of approximation (like "about") but chose to omit them in the final claim, the term must be read narrowly.



##### 21-1729 Expansion
- Judge Taranto dissented, arguing that the #standard-significant-figure-convention—the label—should override the functional rewrite.

## Case: 24-1990 — Jacobson v. United States

*Condensed treatment*
- #FIAFEA whistleblower non-reviewability under 12 U.S.C. § 4208. 
	- See Mr. C SR pass for the doctrinal re-anchor to jurisdiction-stripping + Tucker Act Due Process.


### Rough Notes (JR)

#### Issue

Does the jurisdiction-stripping language in FIAFEA § 4208 provide a clear enough signal to bypass the presumption of judicial review, and if so, does the court need to first perform a _Fisher_ money-mandating analysis before dismissing the case?



#### Facts

Background:
She worked as a mortgage loan officer at Wells Fargo from 1998 to 2007.
March 2012, Elizabeth M. Jacobson sued the United States under the Financial Institutions Anti-Fraud Enforcement Act (FIAFEA). 
The trial court dismissed her claim under the reasons:
1. her declaration did not contain one new factual element necessary to establish the government's prima facie case against Wells
2. She admitted to participating in the fradulent conduct
3. the allegations contined in her declaration we publically disclosed (No new evidence of whistleblowing)

Six months later, a two billion USD settlement was agreed to between Wells and the government. 

In April 2022, Jacobson challenged before  the Court of Federal Claims (CFC) for the prior dismissal denying what was fradulent acts that were in scope were not part of what was in the settlement. She claims under 12 U.S.C. § 4208 the court is required to listen to her case under the *Due Process Clause*  and she should be awarded for share in the civil penalty awarded. 

In May 2024 the courts dismissed her case, stating the CFC lacks jurisdiction. She must bring her case to the FIAFEA. 
- FIAFEA § 4208 explicitly states that "notwithstanding any other law, no court shall have jurisdiction," with only a single narrow enumerated exception

Jacobson appealed and this court determined it has jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1295(a)(3).


#### Reasoning

Analysis:
The court relied on two Supreme Court anchors. 
- First, under _Cuozzo_ and _Block_, there is a **strong presumption of judicial review** that Congress can only rebut with a **"clear and convincing"** indication of intent to bar review. 

- Second, under _Ruhrgas_ and _Sinochem_, courts face **"no unyielding jurisdictional hierarchy,"** meaning a court can pick which threshold ground to dismiss on as long as it does not reach the merits without jurisdiction

Reasoning/Application:
The court operated the Cuozzo rule as a classic **presumption-plus-rebuttal mechanism**. 
- The sweeping statutory language of § 4208 ("notwithstanding any other law") easily met the "clear and convincing" threshold to rebut the presumption of judicial review. Because the court was presented with this independent jurisdiction-stripping statute, it did not need to sequence its analysis with the _Fisher_ money-mandating test. 

The court clarified that the underlying function of the _Fisher_ rule is to sequence cases where the jurisdictional question and the merits question **blend** together. - Since an independent statute already stripped jurisdiction entirely, there was no blend, meaning _Fisher_ did not apply.



### Points for SR (JR)

- [[24-1990]] — *Questions for Mr. C.*

- Jacobson was given no chance to see if she should be awarded for damages under the settlement agreed upon by Wells and the government. Would there have been any path for her to get awarded for damages if the court determined they can measure the merits? 
	- It seems like taking part in a crime should auto-release any good-will whistleblowing would provide even if what was settled was outside the time frame when she was taking part.

## Case: 24-2242 — Ereğli Demir ve Çelik (Erdemir) v. ITC (International Trade Commission)


consolidating three appeals:
- Sunset review
- Changed-circumstances review, 
- Residual jurisdiction  (§ 1581(i))


#P13
*Sufficient-Assumption / conditional-direction* 


### Rough Notes (JR)

*Condensed Treatment*

#### Issue

Can foreign producers who missed the 30-day window for challenging an original ITC (19 USC 1330) material-injury determination reopen that determination via sunset review or changed-circumstances review or reconsideration? (Federal Circuit answers: no on all three paths.)


#### Facts

Erdemir + Çolakoğlu were mandatory respondents in 2016 antidumping-duty investigation on hot-rolled steel from Turkey. Erdemir did NOT challenge the 2016 ITC material-injury determination within 30 days. Çolakoğlu did challenge — separately, on Commerce's dumping determination — and won in 2020 (0% margin, excluded from AD order). Erdemir then argued Çolakoğlu's exclusion made Turkish imports "negligible" and tried three paths to unwind the 2016 ITC determination: reconsideration, changed-circumstances review, and sunset review. All three denied by ITC; all three affirmed by Federal Circuit.>


#### Reasoning

**Ruling**
Erdemir's request for reconsideration of the 2016 Commission determination was — in its "true nature" — a challenge to that determination itself. The 30-day timing framework of 28 U.S.C. § 1581(c) applied. A **protective filing + stay** (pending Çolakoğlu's parallel case) was available to Erdemir in 2016. Erdemir did not use it. § 1581(i) residual jurisdiction is therefore unavailable.

**Sunset review — the essence (top-to-bottom JR)**
> """The consolidated opinion's holdings all rest on a single principle: once Congress has set a statutory timing/procedural framework for challenging a determination, litigants cannot escape that framework by reframing the challenge, invoking residual jurisdiction, or seeking a backward-looking reassessment through a forward-looking review mechanism.""" - Panel

**Inverse role-play vs. [[25-1807]] (Insulet v. EOFlow) — JR observation**
This is actually the inverse role-playing for the EOFlow case. Here, the defendant is actually able to stay on the "hook" for a potential wrong — because reframing does not reformat the original challenge that persists from first claim/injury. Same mechanism (timing rule) as Insulet, different downstream direction: in Insulet, the wrongdoer-in-effect (EOFlow) benefits from Insulet's late filing; here, the challenger-in-effect (Erdemir) is barred from unwinding the earlier ITC determination.

**Key quote**
> """A remedy is not [manifestly] inadequate simply because [a plaintiff] failed to invoke it within the time frame it prescribes.""" — *Juice Farms Inc. v. United States*, 68 F.3d 1344, 1346 (Fed. Cir. 1995)

### Points for SR (JR)

[[24-2242]] — *Questions for Mr. C.*

Taking a break from digestion. To rephrase from Ms. G. 
```
- Did you catch Judge Taranto's phrasing that the ITC **"may (perhaps must)"** treat the past material injury as settled? This is the exact same move the court made in _Insulet_ where they specifically outline the boundaries of a rule but explicitly decline to decide the absolute maximum extreme because it wasn't necessary to resolve the immediate case
```

### Mr. C SR pass

One principle wearing three different opinions — once Congress sets a timing framework, litigants cannot escape it by (a) reframing, (b) invoking residual jurisdiction, or (c) backward-looking reassessment. The **"true nature" doctrine** is [[P13]] at doctrinal scale; the **protective filing rule** is the procedural mirror of [[25-1807]]'s inquiry-notice rule.
