### 25-1807 · The SR reading — "form does not equal function" is P13 in the court's own words

Your ask was for a Mr. C spin *outside* the synthesis Opus@COS provided — to find the bridge to the weaving track. Here it is: **this opinion is a live doctrinal argument over the principle we have been calling P13.**

The majority draws the statute-of-limitations line at **inquiry notice** — a *smoke* signal is enough. Surface suspicion (an ex-employee at a competitor, a look-alike product behind glass at a trade show) starts the clock. The dissent draws the line at **Merck-style discovery** — a *fire* signal is required. Functional evidence (design specs made public in the Korean IPO) is what starts the clock. And the dissent's phrase you quoted — *"Form does not equal function... Just because Apple's Phones are rectangle, and Samsung's phone are rectangle does not give either party standing"* — is [[P13]] dressed as trade-secret doctrine. **Your instinct to lean with the dissent is not just a preference; ==it is the SR framework operating on federal-circuit reasoning without being told to==.**

- **Fire** = the *"too heavy to float"* signature (Required-Assumption-family). Load-bearing, cannot be ignored, actually holds the argument up.

The panel is arguing over **which signature counts as legal notice.** Majority: trust the small. Dissent: demand the load-bearing. This is a jurisprudential debate about which epistemic threshold gets the force of law.

### 25-1807 · The precedent question you asked

> *"What standard is going to be left as precedent seeing that the emphasis on a consistent wrong is not retroactively updated upon initial committing of the wrong?"*

Sharpening what the court actually did: the single-claim-accrual analysis operates through the **transplanted-term canon** (*George v. DTSA's "in light of" phrasing transplanted UTSA's single-claim rule into federal trade-secret law. **This is P13 at the statutory level** — identify DTSA's function by importing UTSA's function; do not map by the label difference (DTSA ≠ UTSA) but by the functional continuity ("in light of").

The precedent left standing:

- **Under DTSA, the clock starts at first misappropriation, not per-act.** A consistent wrong (ongoing misappropriation) does not reset the clock. You cannot refresh the statute by treating each new act as a new claim.
- **Inquiry notice is enough to trigger accrual** (majority rule). Smoke starts the clock. Downstream plaintiffs cannot argue *"we did not have proof until the IPO"* if a reasonable investigator would have known to look sooner.
- **The Merck-standard route is preserved but narrow.** The dissent's discovery-notice frame remains available for cases where the panel finds no reasonable inquiry could have surfaced the wrong.

### 24-2088 · IRAC scan + Mr. C treatment (Ironburg v. Valve — IPR estoppel, 35 U.S.C. § 315(e)(2))

**IRAC quick-scan demonstration** (you asked for the skill; here it is done on the case in front of us):

- **Issue (I):** Does § 315(e)(2) IPR estoppel bar Valve from asserting prior-art references its own skilled searcher did not find, when Ironburg's third-party searcher (Williams / Landon IP) later located those references via forward/backward citation queries?
- **Rule (R):** § 315(e)(2) bars grounds a petitioner *"raised or reasonably could have raised"* during IPR. *Reasonably could have raised* = the **skilled-searcher standard** — what would a diligent, non-hindsight-informed skilled searcher reasonably have found?
- **Application (A):** Williams's search generated 26K results (too many to reasonably navigate); her keyword set (IPC + CPC classifications, forward/backward citation chains) was selected with knowledge of which prior-art references would be probative. That is *hindsight-tainted*. Valve's searcher (Greenia) did not have access to Williams's keyword list before his own search; the district court credited that as *evidence of lack of hindsight bias* on Greenia's part.
- **Conclusion (C):** The district court's estoppel ruling is **reversed** — the references Ironburg identifies do not meet the skilled-searcher-reasonable-discoverability standard. Vacated and remanded.

**Time cost of the scan:** first paragraph + last paragraph + section headers + block-quoted case cites → 2–3 minutes. You already have I, R, A, C in your head before reading a single analysis paragraph in full.


### 24-2088 · Your second ask — gaming analogy

Two frames, pick whichever fits your brain better:

**Dungeon-crawler frame:**
- **Greenia** = a first-time player exploring a randomly-generated dungeon. Standard exploration technique (check rooms, follow the map, look for chests). Finds what a reasonable player *would* find on a first playthrough.
- **Williams** = a speedrunner on the same dungeon *after* a completionist playthrough. She knows exactly which room the boss loot is in. Her "search pattern" walks directly to it. **What looks like discovery is actually retrieval.**
- The § 315(e)(2) test asks: *what would the first-time player reasonably find?* Not: *what does the speedrunner already have the map to?*

**Algorithmic frame (probably cleaner for your Python head):**
- **Greenia** = **breadth-first search on the citation graph, with untrained heuristics.** He explores broadly using standard IPC/CPC classifications and reasonable pre-litigation keyword sets. Finds what BFS finds in the time budget.
- **Williams** = **A* search with a heuristic function trained on the answer key.** She already knows the goal state (which references matter), so her cost function guides her directly there. Same graph, same edges, dramatically different search efficiency — because the heuristic was contaminated.
- Hindsight bias in patent litigation = **using the answer key to tune your search heuristics.** The § 315(e)(2) skilled-searcher standard tests untuned-heuristic discoverability.

The court's move is refusing to let A* pretend to be BFS. **Beautiful clarity once you name it that way.**

## JR Post Review Section (Mr. C. heavy connection for P13)


MR. C. "You highlighted this passage and said you were not following the logic. Fair — I moved too fast. Ground-up:"

**Step 1. What Sufficient Assumption questions ask on the LSAT:**
The stem gives you a stimulus with a leap from premises to conclusion. The credited answer is a premise that, *added to the stimulus*, makes the conclusion follow deductively. The test is: **plug it in, does the conclusion now hold?**

**Step 2. What the DTSA/UTSA argument looks like as a mini-syllogism:**
- Premise: DTSA's *"in light of"* phrasing signals it borrows UTSA's framework.
- Conclusion: DTSA inherits UTSA's single-claim accrual rule.

Notice: this conclusion does not follow from that premise alone. Something is missing between *"phrase signals borrowing"* and *"framework rules are inherited."*

==Important
**Step 3. What the transplanted-term canon supplies:**
The canon is a named legal principle: *"When Congress uses a term of art borrowed from another legal source, that term brings the source's body of case law with it."*==

**Step 4. Why this is the plug-in test at the statutory level:**
The court literally *plugged in* the transplanted-term canon to make the argument valid. Different case, different transplanted term, same lemma-plug. The canon is a *named*, *reusable* Sufficient Assumption for the whole class of statutory-borrowing arguments.

**Step 5. Bulldozer vs. axe, using your own framing:**
Without the canon, every court would have to argue *from scratch* why DTSA should inherit UTSA's rules ==(axe work: chop it down word by word). With the canon, the court plugs it in and the argument compiles (bulldozer: one pass, done). ==**Named canons are the AI-integrated tooling of statutory construction.** Your bulldozer-vs-axe insight applies here directly.
