# Field Clerk Draft — 24-1990 (Jacobson v. US)

## SR framing — the doctrinal punchline

Jacobson is the tightest presumption-plus-rebuttal case in the cohort. Two Supreme Court moves anchor the whole opinion:

1. *Cuozzo* / *Block* — there is a **strong presumption of judicial review**; Congress can rebut it, but only with a **clear and convincing** indication of intent to bar review.
2. *Ruhrgas* / *Sinochem* — courts face **no unyielding jurisdictional hierarchy**; a court can pick which threshold ground to reach for "denying audience," so long as it doesn't reach merits without jurisdiction.

The Federal Circuit takes those two rules and does one clean move: FIAFEA § 4208 ("notwithstanding any other law, no court shall have jurisdiction") clears the *Cuozzo* clear-and-convincing bar; that bar means the CFC does not need to first perform the *Fisher* money-mandating analysis (which is what Jacobson wanted, because it would have forced merits-adjacent inquiry as a stepping-stone).

Two paragraphs, one holding, no dissent. Clean.

## The move worth learning — the Sufficient Assumption structure of a rebuttable presumption

Every rebuttable-presumption doctrine is a **Sufficient Assumption exercise**. The stimulus contains a general rule + a rebuttal condition. The rebuttal condition specifies what strength of signal is needed to overcome the presumption. If the signal meets the threshold, the presumption falls; if it doesn't, the presumption stands.

Here:
- **Stimulus:** courts presume judicial review is available.
- **Rebuttal condition:** clear and convincing indication of congressional intent to bar review.
- **Facts:** § 4208 says "notwithstanding any other law, no court shall have jurisdiction," with a single narrow enumerated exception.
- **Missing premise (the plug-in):** language of this strength IS clear and convincing.
- **Conclusion:** the presumption falls; jurisdiction is barred.

**Practice the exercise:** find three other rebuttable presumptions in the LSAT / legal-reasoning space. Each has the same shape. What differs is the strength-of-signal threshold. "Clear and convincing" (Jacobson) vs. "clear statement" (federalism cases) vs. "beyond a reasonable doubt" (criminal) vs. "preponderance" (civil). **Calibrating the threshold to the domain is the master skill.**

## The Fisher-distinguishing as I-lane discipline

The move I want you to notice is small but load-bearing. Jacobson wanted the CFC to first analyze whether FIAFEA is *money-mandating* (per *Fisher v. United States*) before dismissing on jurisdiction. The panel's response: *Fisher*'s rule addresses cases where jurisdiction and merits *blend* — where the money-mandating question IS the merits question. In this case, an independent statute has already stripped jurisdiction. *Fisher*'s ordering rule does not reach here.

**This is Lane I work at its purest.** Identifying what a rule is FOR — the underlying doctrinal problem it addresses — is often more important than knowing the rule verbatim. *Fisher* is for jurisdiction-merits-blend cases. When there is no blend, *Fisher* has nothing to sequence.
